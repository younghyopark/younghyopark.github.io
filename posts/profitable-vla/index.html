Profitable VLA: Thinking about VLA wearing a business hat

2026-04-22 · Younghyo Park

1. Introduction

Lately there's been a pretty striking race toward a generalist VLA — Physical Intelligence, Skild AI, Generalist AI, and others all trying to build a single model that does everything. I find myself genuinely excited about it as a research direction, but I've also started asking a much more boring question:

how do I imagine these models actually being used to make profit?

This might sound like a separate concern from the research itself, but I don't think it is. The way a VLA gets deployed and sold quietly dictates what technical capabilities it actually needs — and thinking carefully about this could help point the research community in more productive directions. It might even raise the question of whether pretraining a general-purpose VLA is the right approach at all.

So this post is my attempt to work through that. I'm sure I'm getting some of it wrong — if you think I am, I'd genuinely like to hear about it.

2. How LLM companies make money

Before guessing where the VLA money comes from, it's worth looking at where the LLM money comes from. Very broadly, revenue for the big labs splits into three buckets:

  • B2C Subscriptions — ChatGPT Plus, Claude Max, Claude Code, Codex. Individuals paying a flat monthly fee.
  • B2B Pay-Per-Token — the API business. Other companies build products or services on top and pay per token consumed.
  • B2B Custom Model — block deals where the provider trains or heavily tunes a model for a specific company or government.

And the revenue mix across these buckets, based on rough public reporting, looks something like:

B2C Subscriptions B2B Pay-Per-Token B2B Custom Model
OpenAI~80%~15%~5%
Anthropic~25%~70%~5%
VLA????

The natural question is what that last row looks like for VLA. Before guessing the percentages, though, we need to ask a more basic question: what kind of company is training the VLA in the first place?

3. Two archetypes

In my head, there are really two very different kinds of companies that could be training a generalist VLA. The distinction sounds subtle but ends up driving almost everything else.

The first archetype is a VLA Model Provider — a company that trains a general-purpose VLA and sells access to it. They don't necessarily have a robot product of their own. The model is the product. This is the archetype that maps directly onto the LLM table above.

The second archetype is an In-House VLA Powered Product/Service Company — a company that trains a general-purpose VLA internally and embeds it in a product or service they sell directly to end users. The model is a means to a product. The VLA here is infrastructure, not revenue.

These aren't mutually exclusive — a company could do both, or start as one and drift into the other. But I think the distinction is useful because each archetype implies very different answers to "how is the model deployed?", "who fine-tunes it?", and "what does the revenue model look like?".

Let me start with the first archetype, because that's where most of the structural questions live.

4. Path A — VLA Model Provider

If you're a VLA Model Provider, the model itself is your product. Which means before you can even think about revenue, you have to answer a very uncomfortable question.

How do you provide the weights?

For "VLA Model Provider" to make sense as a business, it's really important to protect the weights. Handing a customer access to the model can't look like "hey, thanks for the purchase, here's the download link." Broadly, there are three options.

Option 1: legal enforcement. Rely on contracts to prevent redistribution, the way entertainment businesses (Disney, music labels, etc.) rely on copyright law. This works poorly for any technology that can be copied bit-for-bit, and historically has been a rough deal once your product shows up on the open internet.

Option 2: hardware security. Ship sealed devices with the weights locked behind a secure enclave or software license — think of a Jetson Orin with the model bound to specific hardware. The edge AI security industry is working on this (secure enclaves, ARM TrustZone, NVIDIA's confidential computing, Kudelski's solutions), but as of today there's no fully secure way to deploy model weights on edge devices against a determined attacker with physical access.

Option 3: cloud inference only. Never let the weights leave your servers in the first place — customers call an API, you return predictions. This is where the LLM industry converged, and for good reason: it's the only one of the three that gives you real control.

The first two are a risky bet if a company's revenue model relies entirely on having a proprietary model. Weights on someone else's hardware are fundamentally hard to protect. So if you're following the LLM playbook, cloud inference is the direction you end up going. But that leads directly into the second concern.

The bandwidth problem

If cloud hosting becomes a strict requirement, then every action prediction is subject to network round-trip latency and jitter. Here's roughly where the fastest cloud LLM APIs sit today, measured as Time-To-First-Token (TTFT) for a ~3k-token input, from tokenmix.ai's benchmark:

Model p50 p95 p99
GPT 5.4450ms1200ms2100ms
Sonnet 4.6500ms900ms1400ms
Gemini Pro600ms1800ms3500ms

The p50 numbers are okay for a typing assistant. The p99 numbers are the problem: 1.4–3.5 seconds, i.e. 0.2–0.7 Hz worst-case. I (a human) can wait for an occasionally slow Claude response. Physics will not.

VLAs aren't used for low-level 1 kHz motor control — that's handled by a local controller. But they do sit at the top of a real-time control stack and need to push out desired SE(3) poses or joint positions at roughly 10–50 Hz, which gets handed down to joint position controllers (500 Hz–1 kHz), torque controllers (30–40 kHz), and eventually the motor itself. A p99 of 2 seconds at the very top of that stack blows the budget, and any network hiccup means a missed control cycle.

One common reaction is: fine, make the cloud model output only the high-level stuff — 3D keypoints, SE(3) targets, plans, something MolmoPoint-flavored — and let a smaller local model (the "physical intelligence" layer) consume that and generate the fast joint-level actions. As an architecture, that's reasonable. But as a business, I'd push back as a customer:

Really? I'm paying you huge money for a VLA, and you're telling me I need to bring my own physical intelligence?

At that point the provider has effectively retreated to selling a semantic layer and offloaded the hard part to the customer. That's a much narrower pitch than "we sell you a VLA," and it collapses the list of companies for whom this is a full solution.

Still, for the sake of continuing the discussion, let's assume we somehow figure out a way to guarantee >10 Hz TTFT for cloud-hosted VLAs. What does the revenue mix actually look like then? Let's walk through the three LLM buckets, one at a time.

4a. B2C Subscriptions

The LLM analogue is that people pay $20–$200 per month for ChatGPT Plus or Claude Max. The VLA analogue would be some people paying $20–$200 per month for pi-0.7 or GEN-1 or whatever the consumer-facing VLA subscription ends up being called.

For the LLM version, the target demographic is anyone who owns a phone or laptop (really, any GUI), and the requirement on the model is that it provide real value to most people out of the box — no task-specific finetuning, just prompt it and go.

Translating that to VLA, the target demographic becomes anyone who owns a robot (or any physical embodiment), and the requirement is that the model perform any task in any environment, out of the box, without task/environment-specific finetuning. Which makes the prerequisites for this market to even exist pretty demanding: everyone has to have a personal robot, and models have to be able to complete most tasks via in-context learning alone.

If and when that market exists, the winner is decided by the same two things that decide the LLM winner: how easy it is to prompt, and how performant it is. For LLMs we mostly talk about this as "vibes" — does the model feel good to use. For VLAs, "how easy it is to prompt" is actually a whole axis by itself, because the prompts are much more than text. Very roughly, from easiest-to-provide to hardest: simple language instructions, detailed step-by-step language instructions, task-specific human videos, and task-specific teleoperation data. (That last one isn't as infeasible as it sounds, since we're already assuming everyone has a robot.)

You can imagine the competitive landscape that comes out of this. Company A charges $20/month but their model only really works with one-line language prompts. Company B charges $25/month with a more performant model that expects detailed step-by-step instructions. Company D charges $18/month but bundles in a rental for a wearable prompt-collection device so they can get higher-quality demonstrations. Each company is making a different bet on the "ease of prompting vs. performance" frontier.

If you take this market seriously, two agendas fall out naturally. On the business side, somebody needs to make a robot product that is (a) not proprietary, so users can run different VLA APIs on it, and (b) general-enough hardware to be worth having. On the research side, we need much better in-context learning for VLAs — especially with rich, multi-modal prompts, not just text.

4b. B2B Pay-Per-Token

In the LLM world, the pay-per-token bucket is really two very different customer groups pressed into the same pricing model.

One group is product builders — companies that ship their own product with the model (queried via API) embedded as an invisible component. The end user interacts with the product, not the model. API usage scales with the product's user base and engagement. Cursor, Notion, DoorDash, Duolingo, and many others fall in here.

The other is implementation partners — system integration firms (Accenture, Deloitte, McKinsey, BCG, and similar) that build custom AI-based solutions for enterprise clients using the provider's API. They act as a distribution channel for the model provider. The client pays the partner a project or retainer fee; the partner passes some of that through to the API cost.

Would a robot product company actually be a product builder here?

Any company developing robot hardware as an end-product basically has to make a call:

  1. Build and train an in-house VLA for my robot, or
  2. Just pay the API cost to a provider, or
  3. If (2), should I let the product swap between different providers, or lock to one?

My personal bet is that most serious robot product companies end up at (1), in-house. Two reasons.

Safety and liability. Most LLM applications are reversible at their core. If Claude writes nonsense, I can hit revert, fix the prompt, try again. Physics doesn't have an undo. A robot product company bears legal responsibility when their product causes harm, and depending on an opaque third-party model for safety-critical, irreversible physical actions is a clear no for me.

Margins and control. A product company's whole business is shipping a product that works over many years. Paying per-token forever for the part that decides what the robot actually does — where every outage, price change, or silent model update becomes your problem — is an uncomfortable place to be. LLM-powered products like Cursor have been absorbing exactly this pain as API pricing shifts under them, and I'd expect robot product companies to be especially allergic to it.

Would an implementation-partner-style player buy?

The robotics analogue of Accenture/Deloitte already exists: automation solution providers like Berkshire Grey, Mujin, RightHand Robotics, Locus Robotics, Bastian Solutions, and similar firms. Their model has always been "a business comes to us, we deliver a bespoke automation system." A hotel asks them to automate towel folding. A warehouse asks them to automate package sorting. For each new project, the question becomes: do I actually need a VLA for this?

My bet here is mostly no, and for an interesting reason. These automation tasks are inherently very specific and custom. The hotel client doesn't need a solution that generalizes to other hotels. The warehouse client doesn't need it to generalize to other warehouses. That cross-task, cross-environment generalization is precisely the strength VLAs are selling — and it's not what these customers are asking for. They'd be paying for a capability they don't use.

The only scenario where it really makes sense for a solutions provider to pay token costs for a third-party cloud VLA, instead of training a smaller custom model from scratch, is if a cloud-hosted VLA ICL-ed from task-specific prompts outperforms a locally-runnable model trained from scratch with task-specific data. That's a high bar, and I'm not confident it gets crossed often enough to build a meaningful pay-per-token business on top of.

4c. B2B Custom Model

The third LLM bucket is block deals for custom models — a government or large enterprise contracts the provider to produce a tuned model just for them. For a VLA provider, the natural translation is that the provider company itself becomes a B2B automation solution provider. Since they have the weights, task-specific finetuning is on the table: finetune on data from the client, serve the result over cloud, and keep the base weights secure.

The tricky part is that the direct competitors in this space are the traditional automation integrators (Berkshire Grey, Mujin, and friends), who have been doing this work for years without a VLA. They have existing customer relationships, long sales cycles mastered, and playbooks for per-client integration that have nothing to do with foundation models. Whoever wins this market is whoever gets the most clients with real automation needs — whoever can deliver the best bang-for-buck per-client solution, not whoever has the most impressive model on a benchmark.

Which is a slightly awkward conclusion for a "VLA Model Provider," because the winning move in this market looks indistinguishable from "be a good traditional robotics integrator that happens to use a VLA internally." The VLA is a means, not an edge.

5. Path B — In-House VLA Powered Product/Service Company

Zooming back out to the original two-archetype picture: all of Section 4 was about the left column. If you watch where my "personal bets" in 4b and 4c keep nudging, though, they all point in the same direction — the serious buyers mostly want to own their stack. Which makes the right column, the in-house VLA, worth taking seriously in its own right.

Figure AI, 1X, Tesla (with Optimus), and others are already heading in this direction. The product is the robot, not the model. The VLA is invisible to the customer — they don't know or care that there's a foundation model inside. This is fundamentally different from Path A: the VLA is a cost center, not a revenue source. Money comes from selling hardware (and possibly software subscriptions for updates and new capabilities), not from selling model access.

What's interesting is that the spec sheet for the VLA changes substantially once you're thinking of it as an internal component of a product rather than a product to be sold on its own.

Cross-embodiment? Probably not a priority. The model is going to be used for a single robot product. You don't need it to work on someone else's arm with a different kinematic tree and a different gripper. Drop that requirement and a lot of research complexity drops with it.

Task scope can be narrowed a lot. Depending on how you position your robot in the market — household chores, warehouse picking, a specific industrial niche — the set of tasks the VLA actually needs to handle is a small slice of "every task a human can do." It's still a hard problem, but a much smaller one.

Both of those bullets quietly push against big chunks of the current generalist-VLA research agenda. A lot of the difficulty in building VLAs today comes from trying to be simultaneously cross-embodiment and cross-task. If the business shape is "one company, one robot, one scoped product," you get to drop both requirements on day one — and that changes what the right research questions even are.

There's a real flywheel to chase here too. When thousands of units are deployed, every interaction generates data: robots failing to pick up an unusual mug, successfully navigating a weird floor layout, getting surprising verbal instructions from a user. The company that figures out how to aggregate fleet data and feed it back into model improvements has a powerful loop: more robots deployed → more data → better model → more robots sold. That's the same argument Tesla has been making about autopilot for years. It comes with its own set of hard problems — in-home privacy, catastrophic forgetting, deciding what data is worth keeping — but they're problems on a concrete product, not a generic benchmark.

6. Wrapping up

I do still believe generalist VLAs are a cool and ambitious research direction. I'm not trying to talk anyone out of them. But thinking seriously about their potential use case might ground us a little better — both in what the technical spec actually is, and in what we're implicitly assuming about how the whole thing gets paid for.

Most of the specific claims above are calls I wouldn't stake too much on. The part I'd defend more firmly is the framing. "VLA Model Provider" and "In-House VLA Product Company" are not the same business, and they don't need the same model. Figuring out which one you're building probably has to come before figuring out how many action heads it has.
