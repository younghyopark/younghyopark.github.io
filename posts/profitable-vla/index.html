Profitable VLAs: Viewing Vision-Language-Action Models with a (Skeptical) Business Hat

2026-02-24 · Younghyo Park

1. Introduction

I've been thinking a lot about how VLAs will actually be used. Not in a research lab, not on a benchmark — but in a real business, by a real company, making real money.

This might sound like a separate concern from the research itself, but I don't think it is. The way a VLA gets deployed and sold quietly dictates what technical capabilities it actually needs — and thinking carefully about this could help point the research community in more productive directions. It might even raise the question of whether pretraining a general-purpose VLA is the right approach at all.

With that in mind, I want to think through the concrete ways VLAs might be commercialized. I see roughly three motivations a company might have for training a VLA, and each implies a different set of technical requirements. I'm sure I'm getting some of this wrong — if you think I am, I'd genuinely like to hear about it.

One assumption I'm making throughout this post: pure zero-shot deployment is unlikely to be enough. No matter how good the foundation model is, some form of task-specific or environment-specific tuning — whether that's fine-tuning, in-context learning (prompting), or something else — will probably be required to get a VLA working reliably in a new setting.

2. What is a VLA?

A vision-language-action model (VLA) is a multimodal foundation model that takes in visual observations (camera images or video) and a language instruction ("pick up the red cup"), and directly outputs low-level robot actions (joint angles, gripper commands, etc.).

The key idea is that a VLA is pretrained — typically by fine-tuning a large vision-language model (like PaLM-E or Gemini) on a broad dataset of robot trajectories paired with visual observations and language descriptions. This pretraining is meant to give the model a general understanding of how the physical world works: object shapes, spatial relationships, how things move when you push them, what it means to "put something on the table."

The promise is that this pretrained understanding makes it much easier to build a task-specific policy. Instead of collecting thousands of demonstrations and training a model from scratch for every new task, you start from a VLA that already knows a lot about the world, and adapt it to your specific task or environment with relatively little additional data. In the language model world, this is the same logic behind fine-tuning GPT or Claude on a specific domain — you don't train a language model from scratch every time you want a customer support chatbot.

3. Why Train a VLA?

The way I see it, a company might want to train a VLA for three very different reasons:

  • To sell the model itself — a B2B "VLA provider" that builds the model and sells access to it, the way OpenAI or Anthropic sell access to LLMs.
  • To power a general-purpose robot product — a vertically integrated company that builds both the hardware and the software, selling a complete robot directly to end users. The VLA is what makes it work across diverse environments out of the box.
  • To speed up custom robot solutions — a robotics solution provider that builds tailored systems per client. The VLA is one of their internal tools that reduces delivery time.

These motivations aren't mutually exclusive — a company might start with one and shift toward another. But I think the distinction is useful because each motivation implies very different answers to questions like "how does fine-tuning work?" and "where does the model run?" and "what does the revenue model look like?"

Let me go through each one.

4. Motivation A: Selling the Model (B2B)

The first motivation is probably the most familiar one: build the best VLA, then sell access to it. A foundation model provider for robotics, analogous to what OpenAI and Anthropic have built for language. Several companies seem to be heading in this direction.

The analogy to LLMs is natural. Anthropic just hit $30B ARR as of early 2026, with 70–75% coming from API/B2B revenue. OpenAI is at ~$24B ARR, more consumer-heavy via ChatGPT, but with a massive enterprise API business underneath. So the model is proven, at least for language. But the analogy has limits — the LLM B2B model works because there are millions of developers and thousands of companies building on top of language models. The list of robotics companies that would actually buy a VLA is much shorter.

There are two axes that shape how a VLA provider operates, and I think each deserves some discussion on its own before we look at how they interact.

Who is the Customer?

I think there are two quite different types of customers for a VLA provider, and they map to familiar patterns in the LLM world.

Product companies build hardware and sell a complete robot to end users. They need a VLA to make their robot capable, but they don't want to train a foundation model from scratch — that's not their core competency. They want to buy or license a VLA, adapt it to their robot's form factor and target environments, and embed it in every unit they ship. The fine-tuning here is typically a one-time, broad effort — they need the model to work across all kitchens, not one specific kitchen. The LLM analogy is something like Duolingo, which integrated GPT-4 via API to build their "Roleplay" and "Explain My Answer" features — they didn't train a language model themselves, they built a consumer product on top of OpenAI's foundation model.

Solutions companies are robotics service providers. They don't sell a product off the shelf — they build custom automation systems per client. A warehouse company comes to them and says "we need a robot that can pick these specific items off these specific shelves," and the solutions company delivers a working system. The fine-tuning here is per-client, every time — each new project requires adapting the model to a specific task, environment, and set of objects. The LLM analogy is how firms like Accenture and Deloitte have partnered with OpenAI and Anthropic to build custom AI tools for enterprise clients — fine-tuning or configuring Claude and GPT for specific industries and workflows, deploying bespoke solutions per client engagement.

These two customer types have very different relationships with the VLA provider. Product companies are repeat buyers in a limited sense — they might buy the VLA once and use it across millions of units. Solutions companies are repeat buyers in a deeper sense — they come back every time they have a new client, needing a new round of adaptation.

How Do You Provide the Model?

This is the delivery question, and it has profound implications for IP protection, fine-tuning, and the revenue model.

If you ship the base weights, the customer runs inference locally on their own hardware. This is how most robotics deployments work today — you can't have a warehouse robot that stops working when the WiFi drops. But it also means the customer has the weights on their machine. They can fine-tune them, study them, improve on them, or potentially redistribute them. The VLA provider's core IP is now on someone else's hardware.

The edge AI security industry is working on protecting weights on-device — secure enclaves, ARM TrustZone, NVIDIA's confidential computing, Kudelski's secure enclave solutions — but as of now, there is no fully secure way to deploy model weights on edge devices against a determined attacker with physical access. Companies rely on legal protection plus technical speed bumps, not true cryptographic guarantees.

If you don't ship the base weights, you keep them under your control. This could mean delivering via embedded hardware (a Jetson Orin with the model locked behind a security key — more secure, but now you're in the hardware business), or via a cloud API (the model runs on your servers and returns action predictions — great for IP protection, but latency is a real concern). VLAs aren't used for low-level 1kHz motor control — that's handled by a local controller. But they do need to output actions at roughly 5–30Hz, meaning 33–200ms per prediction. For comparison, today's fastest LLM APIs have a time-to-first-token of ~600ms at best, and that's before accounting for network round-trip. So cloud-based VLA inference is possible in principle, but it's tight — and any network hiccup means a missed control cycle.

The delivery choice also determines who can fine-tune. If the customer has the weights, they can adapt the model themselves. If they don't, the VLA provider is the one doing the fine-tuning — either as an explicit service, or through in-context learning at inference time.

Ship weights + Product company

The product company receives the base weights and can do whatever they want with them — fine-tune, run in-context learning, develop entirely new adaptation techniques. They have full flexibility over the model.

That flexibility is great for the customer, but it's a problem from the provider's perspective. Nothing stops the customer from improving the model beyond what the provider shipped, or from becoming a "second vendor" that resells an enhanced version. What's the sustainable business here? A one-time transaction for the base weights? That's hard to build a company around.

Aside: could derivative models pay royalties up the chain?

One idea worth considering: if model C is fine-tuned on model B, which was fine-tuned on model A — whenever C generates revenue, some fraction trickles back to B and A.

This isn't a new idea in other domains. In music, when you sample someone's track, the original artist typically receives a percentage of copyright ownership in the new song, with ongoing royalties tied to sales and streams. In the patent world, this is known as "royalty stacking" — where a product that builds on multiple patents owes royalties to each patent holder. (Though in practice, royalty stacking often becomes prohibitive and discourages downstream innovation.)

On the enforcement side, there's emerging work on model fingerprinting that can detect whether a model was derived from a specific base model — one approach achieves 94% classification accuracy across model families, even after fine-tuning. And blockchain-based systems have been proposed for automated royalty tracking, where smart contracts could enforce payments whenever a derivative model is used commercially — similar to how ERC-2981 embeds royalty logic into NFTs so original creators get a cut of every secondary sale.

None of this is production-ready for VLAs. But the "ship the weights" model doesn't have to mean "give up all future revenue" — if the industry develops robust provenance tracking and enforceable licensing chains, it could look more like music publishing than a one-time sale. Whether that's realistic is another question.

Don't ship weights + Product company

The VLA provider keeps the weights and fine-tunes once for the product company's use case. The product company doesn't need the VLA adapted to one specific kitchen — they need it to work well across all kitchens. So the provider fine-tunes on a large, diverse dataset that the product company collects internally (or that the provider already has from pretraining), and delivers a model tailored to their robot's form factor and target environment category.

This looks more like a traditional B2B software deal. The data is less sensitive — it's general household or warehouse data, not proprietary processes. The provider could deliver via embedded hardware (a Jetson Orin with the model locked behind a security key) or potentially a cloud API, though latency is tight — VLA action prediction needs to run at 5–30Hz, and today's best cloud API latencies (~600ms to first token) don't leave much room for network overhead.

Ship weights + Solutions company

Similar to the product company case above, but the solutions company fine-tunes per client, every time they take on a new project. They have the same full flexibility — and the same risk to the VLA provider's business.

In fact, the risk is amplified: a solutions company with the base weights and a growing library of fine-tuned variants is essentially building their own VLA capability on top of someone else's foundation. At some point, they might not need the provider at all.

Don't ship weights + Solutions company

This is where it gets most interesting. Fine-tuning happens per client, every time — a solutions company gets a new client who wants to automate a specific task on their factory floor, and they need to collect demonstration data, send it to the VLA provider, and get back an adapted model. Approach D: Fine-tuning as a service

The solutions company (or their end client) collects task-specific demonstration data and sends it to the VLA provider. The provider fine-tunes the model and delivers an updated version adapted to that specific task and environment. The base weights never leave the provider's hands — the customer only receives the fine-tuned result (or access to it via an embedded device or API).

The data collection process becomes a core part of the product experience. The VLA provider needs to tell the customer: here's what data we need, here's how to collect it. And the choice of data modality — teleoperation (high quality, but expensive), egocentric video (cheaper, but hard to extract actions from), exocentric video (lowest barrier, but embodiment gap), or wearable motion capture (middle ground, but requires hardware buy-in) — isn't just a research decision, it's a product decision that shapes the customer experience.

Competitive dynamics. If multiple VLA providers compete in this space, the differentiating factors become: how easy is the data collection, and how good is the resulting fine-tuned model? Company A might claim: "Just shoot 5 iPhone videos of your employees doing the task, and we'll give you a working model." Company B might require a full wearable motion capture kit with a specific collection protocol and a larger number of demonstrations. Company A's pitch is lower friction — but Company B can argue that their approach actually produces a model that works reliably. And Company B might even sell the data collection kit as part of the package, creating an additional revenue stream.

The data privacy tension. This model requires the customer to send task data to the VLA provider. But that data might be confidential — think of a defense contractor or a pharmaceutical company that doesn't want its proprietary manufacturing processes leaving their facility. From the customer's side, there's a real tension between wanting a better model and wanting to keep their data private.

Approach E: In-context learning

This sidesteps the data privacy problem entirely. Instead of sending task data to the provider for fine-tuning, the customer provides a few demonstrations at inference time — no gradient updates, no data leaving the premises. The provider keeps the weights on their own servers. The demonstrations stay local, used only as context for the model's predictions.

This is essentially how LLM APIs work today: you "steer" the model through prompting, not fine-tuning. For product companies, in-context learning is less critical — they're fine with a one-time fine-tuning deal. But for solutions companies with privacy-sensitive clients, it could be the difference between closing a deal and losing it.

If VLAs could reach this level of in-context adaptability, it would be a clean solution to both the IP problem and the data privacy problem. But it's a much harder research challenge for robotics than it was for language, and we're not there yet.

Revenue Model

The revenue model depends on the combination you chose above. Shipping weights naturally leads to one-time licensing, which is simpler but hard to build recurring revenue on. Keeping weights opens up subscriptions, per-query pricing, or per-project fees.

In the LLM world, it's mostly subscriptions and pay-per-token. But robots are different — a factory that buys a robot expects it to work for 10 years without paying a monthly fee to keep the brain running. A subscription model creates recurring revenue but risks customer pushback — especially in industrial settings where "the robot stops working if we stop paying" is a non-starter. Likely some hybrid, but nobody has announced a real pricing model for VLA-as-a-service yet.

5. Motivation B: Powering a General-Purpose Robot (B2C)

The second motivation is different — the company isn't selling a model, it's selling a robot, directly to the end user. You buy it, unbox it, and it works. No setup, no customization, no need to contact the company. Companies like Figure AI, 1X Technologies, and Tesla (Optimus) are pursuing some version of this. They're vertically integrated — the model and the robot are inseparable.

If this is your vision, a VLA isn't just nice to have — it's the only viable path. How else do you build a robot brain that works in every house, every kitchen, every factory configuration?

The Business Model

The product is the robot, not the model. The VLA is invisible to the customer — they don't know or care that there's a foundation model inside. This is fundamentally different from Motivation A: the VLA is a cost center, not a revenue source. It's what makes the robot useful, but the money comes from selling hardware (and possibly a software subscription for updates and new capabilities).

This means the economics are hardware economics. Bill of materials, manufacturing, supply chain, margins on physical goods. The VLA training cost is amortized across every unit sold — which only works if you sell a lot of units. This is part of why these companies have raised large rounds: you need capital both for the data collection and model training, and for the hardware side of the business.

The Generalization Bar

The generalization bar here is extremely high. There's no per-customer solution. The model ships once and has to handle every environment it's placed in — every mug shape, every table height, every floor texture. This requires an enormous amount of diverse real-world training data, and the data collection alone is a significant long-term investment.

But there's a subtlety here. "Works out of the box" doesn't necessarily mean "zero-shot handles every possible task." It might mean the robot ships with a strong general baseline, and the customer teaches it new things over time through natural interaction. The iPhone didn't ship with every app preinstalled — it shipped with a platform that made it easy to add capability. A general-purpose robot might work the same way.

Fine-Tuning Still Happens

Even an "out of the box" robot will encounter novel situations that require adaptation. The question is whether this can be made so frictionless that the customer doesn't think of it as fine-tuning at all — you just show the robot what you want, and it picks it up.

This makes the UX of adaptation as much a research problem as the learning algorithm itself. Teleoperation and wearables are out of the question for a consumer. The model needs to learn from whatever a normal person can naturally provide — showing the robot, verbal instruction, or just a few repeated attempts. If the research community can crack this, it becomes a major competitive advantage for the robot product: "our robot learns from you" is a compelling pitch.

This is also where the distinction between on-device fine-tuning and in-context learning matters. On-device fine-tuning (updating weights locally on the robot's hardware) means the robot permanently learns from experience, but requires compute and careful handling of catastrophic forgetting. In-context learning (adapting at inference time without weight updates) is lighter-weight but ephemeral — the robot doesn't "remember" between sessions unless you persist the demonstrations somehow.

Fleet Learning and the Data Flywheel

When thousands of robots are deployed in homes and factories, every interaction generates data. A robot that fails to pick up a new type of mug, a robot that successfully navigates an unusual floor layout — all of this is potential training signal.

The company that figures out how to aggregate this fleet data and feed it back into model improvements has a powerful flywheel: more robots deployed → more data → better model → more robots sold. Tesla is betting on this with Optimus, and it's the same logic behind Tesla's autopilot data advantage.

But fleet learning raises its own questions. How do you handle data privacy when the robot is recording inside someone's home? How do you aggregate data across diverse environments without the model regressing on previously solved tasks? Research on continual learning, federated learning, and selective data aggregation becomes critical here.

Revenue and Sustainability

The revenue model is more straightforward than Motivation A: you sell robots. But "sell robots" hides a lot of complexity. Is it a one-time purchase or a subscription? If the robot gets better over time through software updates (powered by fleet learning), that's a strong argument for a subscription component. If the robot is a static product that works as-is at purchase, it's harder to justify ongoing fees.

There's also the question of margins. Hardware margins in consumer electronics are thin — typically 30–40% for premium products. If the VLA training cost is high and the unit volume is low (which it will be in the early years), the unit economics might not work. This is the classic hardware startup trap: you need scale to be profitable, but you need profitability (or patient capital) to reach scale.

6. Motivation C: Speeding Up Custom Solutions

There's a third path, and it might be the closest to reality for most robotics companies today. "Submit an inquiry. We'll build a robot solution tailored to your needs."

In this model, the company isn't selling a general-purpose robot and it isn't selling a foundation model. It's selling outcomes — a working robot system customized for each client's specific task. The VLA is an internal tool, a pretrained model that reduces the lead time from "new client signs a contract" to "robot is operational on their floor." Instead of spending 6 months collecting task-specific data and training from scratch, you fine-tune your internal VLA on a few hours of demonstrations and get something working in weeks.

The Business Model

This is a services business, not a product business. Revenue comes from project fees, deployment contracts, and ongoing maintenance agreements. The VLA doesn't generate revenue directly — it reduces the cost and time of delivering each project.

The economics here are fundamentally different from Motivation A or B. In Motivation A, you train one model and sell it many times — near-zero marginal cost per customer. In Motivation B, you train one model and embed it in many robots — marginal cost is the hardware. In Motivation C, every customer requires custom work — the VLA reduces the effort, but doesn't eliminate it. This means the business scales linearly with headcount, not exponentially with model quality.

That said, there's a compounding effect: every project the company completes adds to their internal dataset. Over time, the VLA gets better at handling the kinds of tasks their clients typically ask for. A company that has deployed 200 warehouse picking solutions has a significant data advantage over a competitor starting from scratch — even if they're using the same base VLA. The moat isn't the model, it's the accumulated task data and the operational know-how to deploy it.

But Do You Even Need a VLA?

This is the uncomfortable question for Motivation C.

Pete Florence — formerly of Google DeepMind, now CEO of Generalist AI — trained their GEN-1 model on 500K+ hours of real-world data from scratch, not by fine-tuning a pretrained VLM. His view:

"No amount of downloading data from the internet, by itself, will create the level of fast, fluid, precise, reactive layer of intelligence needed for interacting with the physical world."

For a well-resourced team with enough data for their specific domain, a pretrained VLA might not even help. Training a task-specific policy from scratch (or from a vision backbone like DINOv2) can outperform a general VLA that was trained on everything but your exact use case.

And sometimes the answer isn't even a learned policy at all. A specialized gripper, a cleverly designed fixture, a constrained workspace — mechanical engineering can eliminate the need for the kind of generalization that VLAs provide. If you can make the problem easier through hardware design, a simple classical controller might outperform the most sophisticated VLA.

So the value of a pretrained VLA for a solutions company depends on a crossover point: how much task-specific data do you have? If you're taking on a completely novel task with only a handful of demonstrations, the pretrained VLA's general knowledge is invaluable. If you have thousands of demonstrations from a well-defined, repeatable task, training from scratch might be better. Most real projects probably fall somewhere in between, and the honest answer is that we don't yet have a good sense of where that crossover point is.

The Slippery Slope to "Traditional Robotics"

This is where Motivation C starts to look similar to what already exists. Companies like Berkshire Grey (acquired by SoftBank Robotics in 2023), Plus One Robotics, RightHand Robotics, Locus Robotics (acquired by Zebra Technologies), Mujin, and Dexterity.ai have been doing this for years — custom robotic solutions, per-client integration, long sales cycles, maintenance contracts.

The promise of the VLA-powered solutions company is that it can deliver faster and cheaper because the pretrained model reduces engineering effort. But if the pretrained model doesn't help that much for narrow, well-defined tasks, and if custom mechanical solutions often work better, what exactly separates a VLA-powered solutions company from a traditional robotics integrator?

The answer might be: not much, and that's not necessarily bad. The traditional robotics services market is large and growing. But it does mean the VLA is a tool that speeds things up, not a moat that keeps competitors out. A solutions company that happens to use a VLA internally isn't fundamentally different from one that doesn't — what matters is the quality of the deployed system, the speed of delivery, and the reliability of ongoing support.

Connection to Motivation A

It's worth noting that Motivation C companies are exactly the "customer type 2" from Motivation A. A solutions company that doesn't want to train its own VLA might buy one from a VLA provider instead — which puts us back in cells C, D, or E from the table above. The line between "motivation to train your own VLA" and "customer of someone else's VLA" is blurry, and a company might move between the two as the technology and their internal capabilities evolve.

7. Open Questions

I don't have clean answers to most of what I've raised here. But I think it's useful to at least name the open questions clearly:

  • As far as I can tell, the business model for VLAs is still very much being figured out. Physical Intelligence open-sourced their weights. Figure and 1X are investing heavily in vertical integration. I haven't seen anyone announce pricing for VLA-as-a-service.
  • The "just works" B2C robot requires a level of zero-shot generalization that we're not close to yet.
  • Cloud-based VLA inference is technically marginal for real-time manipulation, and the hybrid cloud-edge architectures that could make it work are still being figured out.
  • Weight security on edge devices remains an unsolved problem, which complicates any business model that involves shipping weights.
  • For narrow, well-defined tasks, task-specific training or even custom mechanical design can be more effective than a general VLA.

None of this means VLAs are a dead end. But I do think the research community would benefit from thinking more concretely about deployment — not just "can we improve success rate on a benchmark," but questions like "what does the customer's fine-tuning workflow actually look like?" or "can this run on a Jetson Orin at 10Hz?" or "is in-context learning sufficient to avoid shipping weights?"

The technical questions and the business questions are more entangled than they might appear. I think it's worth keeping both in mind.
