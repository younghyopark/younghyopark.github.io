Robotics as Biology: four lessons from a field that's been here before

2026-04-25 · Younghyo Park

1. Why I keep thinking about this

The longer I do robotics research, the more it feels like the wrong fields are loaning us their mental models. We borrow from physics (precise laws, clean derivations, controlled apparatus) and from machine learning (benchmarks, scaling curves, ablations on a fixed test set), and then we wonder why our experiments are so hard to interpret and why findings on one robot in one lab evaporate the moment somebody changes the lighting.

My nagging suspicion is that the field we should actually be borrowing from is biology. Not as metaphor, not as a cute branding exercise, but as a methodological tradition. Biology has spent a hundred years working out exactly the problem we have now: how do you do real science on a complex, high-variance, hard-to-control system that you can't shut down for inspection? You can't make two patches of soil identical. You can't make two cows identical. You can't make two real-world manipulation setups identical either. The whole game is figuring out how to draw valid conclusions in spite of that.

One framing note before I dive in. The argument below is for academic robotics labs, not industry. Industry labs have a different job: ship a product, fast. If one heroic demo on one task gets a humanoid through a customer pilot, that's the right call for them, and the methodological care I'm about to argue for would just be overhead in that context. Doing good science may or may not align with shipping a good product, and when it doesn't, industry should follow the product, not the science.

Academia is supposed to be the part of the field that isn't racing industry on product velocity, because we can't and we shouldn't. Our comparative advantage is supposed to be the careful, generalizable, mechanism-revealing work that industry doesn't have time for. That's what science is, and it's also exactly what biology has spent a hundred years figuring out how to do at scale. If we don't differentiate ourselves from industry on that axis, I'm not sure what academic robotics is even for anymore. So when I say "we" in the rest of this post, I mostly mean the academic "we."

This post is my attempt to summarize what I took away from reading four pieces of biology that I think every academic robotics researcher should know about: Fisher's Design of Experiments, Brenner's C. elegans paper, Schmidt-Nielsen's Scaling, and Levins' Strategy of Model Building. None of them are about robots. All of them are, in a deep way, about us.

2. Why biology, of all places

When people hear "robotics is like biology" they usually translate it as "robots will be living things one day" or "neural nets are like brains." That's not what I mean. What I mean is structural:

  • The system you're studying has enormous uncontrolled variability. Two adjacent plots of the same field yield differently for reasons no one can fully articulate. Two real-world rollouts of the same policy succeed or fail for reasons no one can fully articulate.
  • The treatments interact. A fertilizer that helps one seed variety hurts another. A controller gain that's fine on one task breaks another. Effects don't decompose neatly into a sum of one-factor-at-a-time main effects.
  • Each trial is expensive and slow. You don't get to run a million plots, just like you don't get to run a million real-world rollouts. Compute-bound ML doesn't have to think about this in the same way at all.
  • The thing you actually care about, "does this generalize?", is fundamentally an inductive question: from a finite sample of trials, what can I confidently claim about the population of conditions I haven't tested?

Biology had to confront all of this in the 1920s and 30s. The statistical technology and methodological culture that came out of it is a gift left on the porch. We should pick it up.

Four ideas in particular, one each from four different traditions of twentieth-century biology, are the ones I want to talk about.

3. Lesson 1: Pick your model organism (Brenner, C. elegans)

This is the lesson that comes before all the others, and the one I think the field most quietly avoids.

In the mid-1960s, Sydney Brenner was at the LMB in Cambridge, surrounded by the people who had just cracked the genetic code. The next big problem was obvious: how does a genome specify a nervous system? But the molecular biologists of the day were trying to attack it with the same tools that had worked for protein synthesis, and the targets they had at hand (mouse brain, primate cortex) were hopelessly intractable.

Brenner's move was to switch the organism. After looking for something simpler than the standard models, he picked Caenorhabditis elegans: a 1mm soil nematode with about 600 cells (roughly half of them neurons), a 3½-day life cycle on E. coli, and, crucially, a self-fertilizing hermaphroditic reproductive system that made genetic crosses easy. From his 1974 paper:

"Some eight years ago, when I embarked on this problem, I decided that what was needed was an experimental organism which was suitable for genetical study and in which one could determine the complete structure of the nervous system. Drosophila, with about 105 neurons, is much too large, and, looking for a simpler organism, my choice eventually settled on the small nematode, Caenorhabditis elegans."

The criteria for picking the organism were very explicit: small enough to study exhaustively, complex enough that the answers would generalize, and tractable for the technique you're going to use (genetics, in this case). Drosophila was already a famous model organism; Brenner deliberately moved down the complexity ladder to find one even more amenable to the question he wanted to ask.

It worked. The complete cell lineage was traced by Sulston and colleagues by 1983; the connectome of all 302 neurons was published by White, Southgate, Thomson, and Brenner in 1986. Two Nobel prizes have come directly out of worm work (Brenner/Sulston/Horvitz in 2002 for cell-death genetics; Fire/Mello in 2006 for RNAi), and a third (Chalfie's share of the 2008 GFP prize) leaned heavily on it. The whole strategy hinged on the up-front decision of what to study.

The robotics analog is obvious and, I think, weirdly underdone. The field generally picks platforms based on what's available, what looks impressive in videos, or what the lab next door is using. The criterion that actually matters, is this platform tractable enough that we can run the experiments we want to run, while complex enough that the answers will transfer?, almost never gets debated explicitly.

In my own work I keep converging on something like a Franka FR3 with a small set of canonical manipulation tasks (block picking, mug hanging, bimanual handover). Not because they're the most exciting tasks anyone could think of, but because they sit at the right point on the tractability/complexity curve for the questions I care about. That's me, half-accidentally, picking a model organism. If we did this on purpose (declared a small set of platforms and tasks as community-level "model organisms" and accumulated deep, comparable experiments on them across labs), I think we'd buy ourselves about a decade of progress. C. elegans biology is not impressive because every paper studied a new worm. It's impressive because every paper studied the same worm, and the cumulative depth of understanding compounded.

4. Lesson 2: Design experiments to interact, not isolate (Fisher)

Once you have a tractable system, the next question is how to interrogate it. This is where R. A. Fisher's The Design of Experiments (1937) comes in, and it's where most robotics evaluations are at their most embarrassing.

Fisher had to handle a problem that doesn't really exist for ML benchmarks but very much exists for robotics: every experimental trial happens under conditions that are not, and cannot be, identical. Two adjacent plots of soil yield differently. Two real-world rollouts of the same policy succeed or fail for reasons no one can fully articulate. Field experimentation in agriculture had to invent the modern statistical apparatus from scratch to handle this. The same apparatus is sitting right there for us.

Three of his ideas in particular are worth taking from the book directly.

Randomisation as the physical basis of validity.

It is not enough to make conditions "as similar as possible"; they will never actually be similar. What you do instead is randomise assignments, so that uncontrolled differences cannot bias the comparison. In Fisher's words, randomisation is "the only point in the experimental procedure in which the laws of chance, which are to be in exclusive control of our frequency distribution, have been explicitly introduced." Translation for us: when you compare policy A vs. policy B in the real world, they have to be interleaved rollout-by-rollout, with the order seeded and logged. "All A in the morning, then all B in the afternoon" is not a comparison; it's a comparison-of-policies tangled with a comparison-of-everything-else-that-drifted-between-sessions.

Replication for error estimation, not just variance reduction.

When we report success rates over N trials, we usually justify it as "to reduce noise." Fisher is careful to point out that replication serves a second, more important purpose:

"[Replication's] main purpose, which there is no alternative method of achieving, is to supply an estimate of error by which the significance of these comparisons is to be judged."

If your variance bar is computed from 3 seeds, you don't have an error estimate; you have a number. Without a credible estimate of the experiment's own error, the only way for someone else to judge whether a 4-point success-rate gap is real is to lean on their prior intuition about how much rollouts vary in this kind of setup. That makes the result not self-contained, and it can't carry conviction to anyone outside the lab.

Factorial designs over one-at-a-time.

This is the lesson I most want to put on a billboard. Fisher devotes a whole chapter to dismantling the "scientific" myth of varying one factor at a time:

"In expositions of the scientific use of experimentation it is frequent to find an excessive stress laid on the importance of varying the essential conditions only one at a time. … This ideal doctrine seems to be more nearly related to expositions of elementary physical theory than to laboratory practice in any branch of research."

If you have four binary factors to test, the textbook approach has you do four separate ablations. The factorial approach runs all 16 combinations, randomised, and gets you (a) the same precision on each main effect as a dedicated single-factor study, (b) all the pairwise and higher-order interactions for free. In any system where factors interact (which includes essentially every robot learning pipeline), one-at-a-time systematically misses the most interesting structure. Most of the consequential pieces of robot-learning lore feel like exactly this kind of unmeasured interaction. "Action chunking helps." Helps relative to what action space, what controller stiffness, what data scale? "Diffusion policies beat Gaussian heads." On what task density, what observation space, what training-time budget? Each has been shown one-at-a-time, in isolation, on different setups. We have approximately zero clean factorial evidence about how any of them combine.

What about the combinatorial blowup?

The obvious objection to "just run the factorial cube" is that 2n grows fast and rollouts are expensive. Four factors is 16 cells, fine. Six is 64. Eight is 256. At three reps each you're already at 768 rollouts, which is more than most robotics papers ever do for any single result. So in practice, "go factorial" can sound like a thing that's only theoretically available.

Fisher took this concern seriously and addresses it head-on in the last two sections of the factorial chapter. There are three escape hatches, and a fourth that got formalised after the book but follows directly from his reasoning.

Escape hatch 1: hidden replication means you need fewer per-cell repeats than you think. This is the most underappreciated property of factorial designs and the one most worth internalising. In a 24 = 16-cell experiment, the main effect of factor A is computed as the difference between the 8 cells where A is high and the 8 cells where A is low. Even if each of those 16 cells is run only once, the main effect of A is already an average over 8 trials. So a single rollout per cell gives you 8-fold averaging on every main effect "for free," courtesy of the structure of the design itself. Fisher's phrase: "the loss of absolute replication is made good by the hidden replication inherent in the factorial arrangement." The same is true for pairwise interactions, which average over 4 cells each. The intuition that "I need 5 reps per cell" comes from the one-at-a-time mental model, where each comparison only uses two cells. In a factorial design, that intuition systematically over-counts.

Escape hatch 2: trade absolute replication for more conditions. If you have a fixed budget of, say, 96 rollouts, the textbook impulse is to do 6 reps of 16 cells. Fisher works out the alternative arithmetic: you can also do 2 reps of 48 cells. Same total budget, three times the design space, and the precision on each main effect is unchanged because of the hidden-replication argument. The only thing you lose is a chunk of the degrees of freedom for the error estimate (80 versus 48 in his worked example), which mostly matters at the statistical-significance margin and often doesn't matter for your actual interpretation.

Escape hatch 3: drop replication entirely; use higher-order interactions as your error term. This is Section 41 of the book ("Experiments without Replication") and it's the practical crown jewel. With 6 binary factors = 64 cells, run each cell once. The 63 degrees of freedom decompose as: 6 main effects + 15 pairwise interactions + 20 three-way + 15 four-way + 6 five-way + 1 six-way interaction. The argument: in any reasonable system, three-way and higher interactions are usually too small to matter. So pool those 42 d.f. of high-order interactions and use them as your error estimate. You get clean reads on all the main effects and all the pairwise interactions for the cost of running each cell once. Fisher also names the failure mode and the recovery move: "If, however, contrary to expectation, it appeared that there was a large interaction between two of these factors, it would be advisable to examine separately the interactions between 3 factors which involve these two." In other words: pool aggressively by default, and un-pool selectively when something interesting jumps out.

Escape hatch 4: fractional factorial designs. Fisher gestures at this but the full machinery was developed later by Finney (1945) and Box & Hunter (1950s, the same Box from Statistics for Experimenters). The idea: instead of running all 2n cells, run a carefully chosen 1/2, 1/4, or 1/8 fraction. You give up the ability to distinguish certain interaction terms (they get "confounded" or aliased with each other), but if you've already decided the high-order interactions are negligible, you're not actually losing anything estimable. The payoff is dramatic: with a half-fraction of a 27 design you can probe 7 binary factors in 64 trials instead of 128. With a Plackett-Burman screening design you can probe 7 factors in 8 trials, which sounds like cheating but works for screening which factors are even worth studying further. This is what industrial DOE actually uses in practice, and it's why semiconductor fabs and chemical plants don't run a million-cell factorial when they want to optimise a process.

Putting all of these together, the practical playbook for our setting looks roughly like:

  • Up to ~4 binary factors: run the full 2n cube, with as many reps per cell as you can afford. 16 to 80 rollouts is completely tractable for most groups and gets you everything (main effects, all interactions, a clean error estimate).
  • 5 to 6 factors: run the full 2n once, no replication, and use higher-order interactions as your error term. 32 to 64 rollouts. Same precision on the things you care about; you just give up the ability to detect tiny three-way effects.
  • 7+ factors: switch to a fractional factorial. Most of the factors are probably nearly independent of each other anyway; you just want to figure out which ones matter, and screening designs are purpose-built for that.
  • Continuous factors: bin them to two or three levels (low/high, or low/mid/high) for the screening pass. Once you know which factors matter, do a follow-up with a finer sweep on just those, holding the rest at sensible defaults.

The thing this playbook gets you, in any case, is much more than a one-at-a-time study with the same budget. A six-factor screen at 64 rollouts tells you which two or three factors actually matter and how they interact. A six-factor one-at-a-time study at 64 rollouts tells you the marginal effect of each factor under one specific setting of the others, which is almost always the wrong question. The cost is the same; the information return is roughly an order of magnitude apart.

OK but how do you actually analyse it?

Fair question. With one rollout per cell and no explicit replication, what are you supposed to do with the resulting numbers? The answer is analysis of variance, which is also Fisher's invention and is essentially purpose-built for this situation.

For a 2n factorial design, every effect (main effect or interaction) is a linear contrast: a particular ±1-weighted sum of the cell outcomes. Coding factors A, B, C as ±1 and writing the 8 outcomes as y1 through y8:

  • The main effect of A is (mean of cells where A=+) minus (mean where A=−), which works out to a signed sum where the signs match A's column.
  • The AB interaction is the signed sum where the signs are the product of A's and B's columns.
  • The ABC interaction is the signed sum where the signs are the product of all three columns.

Seven contrasts, seven degrees of freedom, exactly matching the 8 − 1 = 7 d.f. you have in 8 cells. Every cell contributes to every effect. That's the hidden replication made concrete: each main effect is already averaging four observations even though no cell is literally replicated.

The remaining question is how to tell signal from noise without any explicit error estimate. Two standard options.

Option 1: pool the high-order interactions as the error term. This is Fisher's Section 41 move. If you're willing to assume that three-way and higher interactions are negligible (the world rarely has weird "A only matters when B and C are both high" effects), then the measured values of those high-order contrasts are essentially measuring noise: if ABC is "really" zero in the population, the value you compute for ABC from your data is whatever rollout-to-rollout variance happened to load onto that contrast. Pool those terms, divide each effect's mean square by the pooled error mean square, get an F statistic. ANOVA software (statsmodels in Python, base R) does this in one line. In a 24 you get 5 d.f. for error this way; in a 25, 16 d.f. The technique gets really practical for 5+ factors and is a bit dicey for 23.

Option 2: half-normal plot (Daniel, 1959). More visual and honestly what I'd reach for first in a robotics setting. Compute all 2n − 1 effects, take absolute values, sort them, and plot them against the expected order statistics of a half-normal distribution. If every effect were pure noise, the points would lie on a straight line through the origin. Real effects show up as outliers above the line. No a priori error estimate needed: the noise effects estimate the error themselves, by virtue of forming the line, and the real effects sort themselves out by deviating from it. In a 24 with 15 effects you'll typically see 10 to 13 of them lying on a clean line and 2 to 5 of them shooting off above. You don't even need a p-value to read the plot. This is what most experienced DOE practitioners actually use for unreplicated factorials, and Box, Hunter & Hunter spend a lot of pages on it.

One caveat for our setting: strict ANOVA assumes outcomes are roughly normally distributed with constant variance across cells. Robotics success rates aren't either. They're bounded in [0, 1], the variance depends on the mean (a cell at 50% success has more variance than one at 95%), and "success" is binary at the per-trial level. Three pragmatic workarounds:

  1. Logistic regression with the same factorial design matrix. Same effects, same interpretation, but the model respects the binomial structure of the outcome. statsmodels.formula.api.logit('success ~ A * B * C', data=df).fit() gives you coefficients for every main effect and interaction in one call, with confidence intervals you can actually trust.
  2. Bootstrap. Resample trials with replacement, recompute each effect, look at the empirical distribution of effect magnitudes. No parametric assumptions, slow but bulletproof, and works for any test statistic you care to define.
  3. Half-normal plot anyway. It's robust to mild distributional violations and tends to give the right answer for screening, which is what unreplicated factorials are for in the first place.

So the actual end-to-end workflow for a 24 screening pass with one rollout per cell looks like: randomise the order of the 16 cells, run them, compute the 15 signed-contrast effects (about three lines of code), make the half-normal plot, eyeball which effects are real, optionally fit a logistic regression on the same design matrix to get coefficient estimates and intervals, and then follow up at higher replication on whatever the screen flagged as worth chasing. That's it. Same statistical machinery the agronomy stations at Rothamsted were using in 1925, just with NumPy and a laptop.

And the kicker: highly standardised experiments don't strengthen your conclusions, they narrow them.

"The exact standardisation of experimental conditions… always carries with it the real disadvantage that a highly standardised experiment supplies direct information only in respect of the narrow range of conditions achieved by standardisation."

A lighting-controlled, single-extrinsic, single-object-pose experiment doesn't tell you your method works. It tells you it works under those exact conditions. Biology never tries to hold the cow constant; it varies the cow on purpose and absorbs the variation into the error term, so the conclusion comes out generalising across cows. We should be doing the same.

5. Lesson 3: Look for scaling laws, not just point estimates (Schmidt-Nielsen, Milo & Phillips)

Biology spent its first century being almost entirely qualitative. "Gene X affects process Y." "Hormone A regulates organ B." It only matured into quantitative biology when people started measuring things: fast, precisely, and across orders of magnitude.

Knut Schmidt-Nielsen's Scaling: Why is Animal Size So Important? opens with the observation that animals span 1021 in mass between the smallest mycoplasma and the largest whale. That's not a difference of degree; it's a difference of physical regime. And when you ask how a biological function (metabolic rate, heart rate, bone strength, oxygen transport) varies across that range, you find clean scaling laws that hold across phyla. The canonical example, which the broader literature spends decades arguing about, is Kleiber's law: metabolic rate scales roughly as mass3/4 across an extraordinary range of organisms. A single exponent that organises an enormous amount of biology.

Schmidt-Nielsen's other key observation is that many of the things that look like fundamental constraints can be overcome by changing the design, not the quantity. Diffusion alone can supply oxygen to a microorganism, but is hopelessly inadequate for an elephant. The fix isn't more diffusion; it's a new mechanism: convection in blood, plus hemoglobin as a 100× amplifier of oxygen-carrying capacity. The system reorganises around the constraint rather than fighting it. He uses the engineering analogy of stone arches versus suspension bridges: as you scale up, you don't make a bigger stone arch, you switch to a fundamentally different design where the load-bearing elements are in tension instead of compression.

Both of these are exactly the kind of result robotics should be producing and almost never does. We report success rates at single operating points. We almost never plot performance as a function of a continuous design choice over multiple orders of magnitude. When was the last time you saw a robotics paper with a log-log plot of success rate vs. data scale, controller stiffness, or policy frequency, fit with a clean exponent and discussed in terms of why that exponent? When was the last time we said something like "below this threshold the system is bottlenecked by X, but above it the binding constraint switches to Y, so the slope of the curve changes"?

Milo & Phillips, in the preface to Cell Biology by the Numbers, push the same argument from a different angle: biology was held back for decades by being qualitative, and matured when people started making precise quantitative measurements that could be cross-checked across methods. They borrow Lord Kelvin's line (knowledge without measurement is "meagre and unsatisfactory") and propose that biology needs a "cheat sheet" of well-established quantitative facts: how big, how fast, how many, how much. They argue that the act of trying to explain why a number has the value it does becomes an engine of discovery. You can't ask "why is this 0.7" if all you've ever reported is "it works."

This is one of the most underrated opportunities in our field. We should be hunting for the robotics analogues of Kleiber's law: clean numerical relationships that hold across systems and tell you why. "How does sample efficiency scale with controller stiffness?" (We took a first crack at this in our recent Tune to Learn work, where the choice of controller gains turns out to interact strongly with whether you're doing BC, RL, or sim-to-real, and the right setting differs by an order of magnitude across regimes.) "How does sim-to-real gap scale with policy frequency?" "How does success rate scale with demonstration count, and is the exponent the same across embodiments?" These are answerable questions with clean numbers attached, and the field treats them like exotica.

6. Lesson 4: Be honest about what you sacrificed (Levins)

The cleanest single idea in this entire reading list comes from a 10-page paper Richard Levins wrote in 1966 called "The Strategy of Model Building in Population Biology." His argument is elegant and brutal:

"It is of course desirable to work with manageable models which maximize generality, realism, and precision toward the overlapping but not identical goals of understanding, predicting, and modifying nature. But this cannot be done."

You can pick at most two of {generality, realism, precision}. Three strategies fall out of which one you sacrifice:

  1. Sacrifice generality. Build a faithful, precise model of one specific system. (Levins names fishery biologists; we'd name digital twins, or any sysID-then-deploy pipeline.) Predictions are precise but only valid for the exact setup you modeled.
  2. Sacrifice realism. Build clean, general equations and hope the simplifying assumptions don't matter. (Levins names physicists doing population biology; we'd name everyone analyzing toy MDPs or LQR derivations.) Predictions are precise and broad but rest on assumptions that don't actually hold in practice.
  3. Sacrifice precision. Build flexible, often graphical, qualitative models that span many systems but only generate inequalities or signs. (Levins names himself and MacArthur. We don't really have a vibrant analog in robotics, which is part of the problem.) Predictions are general and grounded in reality but only ever qualitative.

The honest paper, in Levins's framework, is the one that names which corner it's playing in. The dishonest paper is the one that quietly pretends to be in all three corners at once.

This maps straight onto our methodological debates. A high-fidelity simulation gives you precision and generality (across initial conditions) but sacrifices realism; the sim-to-real gap is, definitionally, the realism debt. A real-world teleoperated demo collection gives you realism and precision but sacrifices generality; you have data for this environment, not the population of environments you want to cover. An analytical bound or stability theorem gives you generality and realism (it holds for any system in its assumed class) but only in qualitative or worst-case form. None of these are wrong. They are different bets, and they don't compose into "the right way."

Levins also offers a constructive way out, which I think is the deepest thing in the paper. When you have a question that matters, build several models that take different sacrifices, and look for the result that holds across all of them. He calls this a robust theorem:

"…if these models, despite their different assumptions, lead to similar results we have what we can call a robust theorem which is relatively free of the details of the model. Hence our truth is the intersection of independent lies."

If a sim study, a real-world study, and a theoretical analysis all converge on the same conclusion, that conclusion is more credible than any of them on its own, because their failure modes are independent. The current incentive structure in robotics research is roughly the opposite. We pick one corner (usually #1, the high-fidelity-of-one-system corner) and try to wring a paper out of it. The Levins move would be to deliberately attack the same question from multiple corners and report when they agree (and, more interestingly, when they don't).

7. What I'd actually like to see us do differently

These four lessons hang together more than they look like at first. They are all answers to the same underlying question: how do you do science on a complex, high-variance, hard-to-control system that you can't shut down for inspection? Biology has been answering this question for a hundred years. We can either reinvent its answers slowly or read its books quickly.

Concretely, applied to a typical robotics paper:

  • Pick a model organism, and stick to it. State up front what platform and task suite you've chosen and why: tractability, complexity, transferability. Make it easy for other labs to compare against the same setup. Resist the temptation to constantly move to a new robot for novelty.
  • Stop varying one thing at a time. When you ablate, run the factorial cube and report interactions. The interactions are usually the interesting story.
  • Randomise eval order; report variance properly. Three seeds is not an error estimate. Interleave A and B trials. Log the random seed. Treat the rollout-to-rollout variance as a first-class number, not a footnote.
  • Hunt for scaling laws. Plot performance against your continuous design knobs over orders of magnitude. Fit the exponent. Explain it. Don't let "method works at one operating point" be the final form of any result.
  • Name your Levins corner. Tell me whether the result is meant to be precise-and-general (sim), precise-and-realistic (real-world), or general-and-realistic (theory). Better still, attack the same question from two or three corners and look for the robust theorem at the intersection.

None of this is hard. None of it requires new tools. It mostly requires treating the experiment as something to be designed, not just executed.

8. Closing & further reading

If we're going to take the analogy seriously and treat robotics as biology, we should at least read what biology has already worked out. The four-piece starter pack:

  • R. A. Fisher, The Design of Experiments (1937). The methodology bible. Short, opinionated, surprisingly readable. Skip the arithmetic and read it for the arguments.
  • Sydney Brenner, "The Genetics of Caenorhabditis elegans" (Genetics, 1974). ~24 pages. The model-organism move, written by the person making it. Read the introduction even if you skim the genetics tables.
  • Knut Schmidt-Nielsen, Scaling: Why is Animal Size So Important? (1984). Chapters 1 and 2 are enough to absorb the spirit. The rest is depth on specific systems, useful but not load-bearing.
  • Richard Levins, "The Strategy of Model Building in Population Biology" (American Scientist, 1966). Ten pages. The single highest-leverage thing on this list. Read it twice.

Honorable mentions worth knowing about even if you don't read in full:

  • Milo & Phillips, Cell Biology by the Numbers: the quantitative-biology manifesto. The preface alone makes the case in 10 minutes.
  • Horace Freeland Judson, The Eighth Day of Creation: the definitive history of how molecular biology was actually done in the 30s through 70s. Worth reading not for any one technique but to feel what an empirical research culture looks like when it's working at its best.
  • Box, Hunter & Hunter, Statistics for Experimenters: the modern industrial-engineering descendant of Fisher. The chapters on factorial and fractional factorial designs read directly onto how we should structure ablations.
  • John Platt, "Strong Inference" (Science, 1964): the design-experiments-to-kill-hypotheses argument in under ten pages. Pairs nicely with Fisher's null-hypothesis discussion.

I'm not claiming any of this turns robotics into biology overnight. But I do think the field has been borrowing from the wrong neighbours, and the right neighbours have spent a century working out exactly the methodological problems we now have. There's no harm in going to read their notes.
