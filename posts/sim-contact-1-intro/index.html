Contact in 3 Simulators, Part 1 — Intro

2026-05-07 · Younghyo Park
Series Contact in 3 simulators
Part 1 — Intro · Part 2 — MuJoCo · Part 3 — PhysX · Part 4 — Drake
This series came out of curiosity, not advocacy. I wanted to know what actually differs between MuJoCo, PhysX, and Drake when they touch contact — not as marketing, but at the source-code level. This intro lays out what's the same, what's different, and (honestly) where the differences matter and where they don't.

1. Why these three

These three are the simulators most robotics and animation people end up choosing between for rigid-body work that involves contact:

  • MuJoCo. Originated in Todorov's lab; now maintained by Google DeepMind. The dominant choice for contact-rich reinforcement learning and model-predictive control. Source: google-deepmind/mujoco.
  • PhysX. NVIDIA's general-purpose physics engine, originally built for games. Now also the backbone of NVIDIA Isaac Sim / Isaac Lab. Source: NVIDIA-Omniverse/PhysX.
  • Drake. RobotLocomotion / TRI's robotics simulation and design framework. Strong in manipulation research, where its hydroelastic contact model is its defining feature. Source: RobotLocomotion/drake.

Other engines exist (Bullet, ODE, MuJoCo MJX, Brax, Genesis, IsaacGym on top of PhysX). I'm not covering them. The three I picked span the interesting axes — if you understand these, you can place the others.

2. The honest summary, up front

If you read nothing else, this is the takeaway:

  • MuJoCo and PhysX answer the same question. Both treat contact as a constraint: when bodies penetrate, the solver finds whatever forces are needed (right now) to resolve the penetration and respect the friction cone. The forces are Lagrange multipliers, not material outputs. Where they differ is in how the constraint problem is solved — MuJoCo formulates a global convex optimization per island and runs Newton/CG/PGS; PhysX maintains a persistent contact manifold per pair and iterates impulses (PGS or TGS).
  • Drake answers a different question. Hydroelastic contact treats the contact region as a small overlap of two pressure fields, computes the surface where they balance, and integrates the pressure to get a force. The force is prescribed by state and material; Drake's solver only enforces Newton's laws and friction, not the contact force itself. Drake also supports a point-contact model (via FCL), so it can be used in the same family as MuJoCo/PhysX too.
  • "Better" depends on the question. For contact-rich RL with stiff timesteps, MuJoCo wins on speed and stability and has the cleanest convex formulation. For game-style physics where you mostly want plausible behavior at scale, PhysX's persistent manifold + iterative-impulse pipeline is what has been battle-tested for two decades. For manipulation problems where you care about distributed contact, smoothness, and physically-meaningful material parameters — especially if you're considering gradient-based methods — Drake's hydroelastic path is genuinely different and the only one of the three that gives you that.

The rest of this post unpacks why those statements are true. The three deep-dive parts (linked at the bottom) do the actual code walks.

3. Two views of contact

Before getting to the table, the single most useful thing to internalize is that there are two genuinely different ways to think about what contact is, in a physics simulator. MuJoCo and PhysX implement the first; Drake's hydroelastic implements the second.

3.1. Contact-as-constraint

The first view says: contact is a constraint. When two rigid bodies touch, the universe imposes a rule on them — "you cannot interpenetrate" — and the simulator's job is to find whatever forces are needed right now to make the rule hold while everything else in the dynamics keeps doing its thing. The forces themselves are not derived from a material model. They're Lagrange multipliers: whatever values it takes to keep the constraint satisfied. Friction sits on top of this as another constraint ("the tangential force must lie inside the friction cone") that also gets enforced by some impulse the solver works out.

This view is the lineage of Stewart, Trinkle, Anitescu, Potra, Erleben — "rigid body contact dynamics" — and it leads naturally to a constrained optimization problem each timestep: given the bodies' inertias and external forces, find the contact forces that satisfy non-penetration, the friction cone, and Newton's laws. MuJoCo and PhysX both live here. So do Bullet, ODE, and most engines you'd recognize as a "rigid body simulator."

3.2. Contact-as-constitutive-law

The second view says: contact is a constitutive law. The bodies aren't rigid — they're slightly squishy, and what we call "contact" is really a small region where they overlap and push back on each other with some pressure that depends on how much they overlap. Specify the pressure law (a stress-strain relationship of the material), integrate it over the overlapping region, and you get a force. The simulator then plugs this force into the equations of motion the same way it plugs in gravity or a spring.

This view is the lineage of finite element method (FEM) people treating contact as a constitutive boundary condition, and its cleanest modern crystallization is Drake's hydroelastic contact model (Elandt et al. 2019).

3.3. The dual lens

  • Constraint-based contact answers: "given current state and inertia, what set of contact impulses is consistent with non-penetration and the friction cone?" Force is what the solver decides it is.
  • Constitutive contact answers: "given current state, what is the pressure at every point on the contact surface?" Force is fully prescribed by state, and the equations of motion just receive it.

Both are valid. They aren't strictly incompatible — they're dual ways of describing the same phenomenon, and in certain limits they agree. But the engines that grow out of each viewpoint look very different inside.

3.4. What problem does each actually solve?

Both views become precise when you write down the optimization problem the engine actually attacks. Below are the formulations taken verbatim from each codebase — not glossed-over versions. Variable names match what you'd find in MuJoCo's doc/computation/index.rst and Drake's SAP source (multibody/contact_solvers/sap/sap_model.h).

The constraint-based problem (MuJoCo)

MuJoCo's primal problem finds the constrained acceleration \(\dot{v}\) by solving:

\[ \begin{aligned} (\dot{v},\, \dot{\omega}) \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{(x,\, y)}\; & \bigl\lVert x - M^{-1}(\tau - c)\bigr\rVert^{2}_{M} \;+\; \bigl\lVert y - a_{r}\bigr\rVert^{2}_{R^{-1}} \\[4pt] \text{subject to}\quad & J_{\mathcal{E}}\, x_{\mathcal{E}} - y_{\mathcal{E}} = 0 \quad\text{(equalities)} \\ & J_{\mathcal{F}}\, x_{\mathcal{F}} - y_{\mathcal{F}} = 0 \quad\text{(friction loss)} \\ & J_{\mathcal{C}}\, x_{\mathcal{C}} - y_{\mathcal{C}} \in \mathcal{K}^{*} \quad\text{(contacts, dual cone)} \end{aligned} \]

Source: references/mujoco/doc/computation/index.rst, line 1106 (eq. :label: eq:primal).

Reading the symbols:

  • \(x\) is the optimization variable for generalized acceleration; \(y\) is a slack variable in constraint space (its physical interpretation in MuJoCo's docs is the "deformation acceleration" of an augmented system).
  • \(M\) is the inertia matrix, \(\tau\) is the applied generalized force, \(c\) is the bias. \(M^{-1}(\tau - c)\) is the unconstrained "free-flight" acceleration.
  • \(R\) is the diagonal regularizer (computed from solimp); \(a_{r}\) is the reference acceleration (computed from solref). Both are what Part 2 unpacks at length.
  • \(J_{\mathcal{E}}, J_{\mathcal{F}}, J_{\mathcal{C}}\) are the constraint Jacobians for equalities, friction-loss constraints, and contacts.
  • \(\mathcal{K}^{*}\) is the dual of the friction cone (which coincides with the cone itself for the pyramidal case; the elliptic cone has a distinct dual).
  • \(\lVert z \rVert^{2}_{W} = z^{T} W z\), a positive-definite weighted norm.

In words: find the acceleration \(x\) closest in \(M\)-norm to free flight, plus a slack acceleration \(y\) close in \(R^{-1}\)-norm to the reference acceleration \(a_{r}\), subject to per-pair constraints that \(J\,x - y\) sits in the appropriate set (zero for equalities, dual cone for contacts). (Strictly, the second norm is a Huber norm \(\lVert\cdot\rVert^{\text{Huber}(\eta)}_{R^{-1}}\) to handle friction-loss interval bounds; in the absence of friction-loss constraints, both norms are quadratic and the problem is the QP shown above. See MuJoCo's docs eq. eq:primal for the full Huber form.)

Where are the contact forces?

Notice that contact forces don't appear anywhere as a variable in the primal problem above. The optimization is over \((x, y)\) — accelerations — with constraints involving \(J_{\mathcal{E}}, J_{\mathcal{F}}, J_{\mathcal{C}}\) but no explicit force vector. Where did the forces go?

The MuJoCo doc is explicit about this: "We start with a primal problem defined over accelerations \(\dot{v}\) where the constraint forces are implicit. We then transform the primal problem over accelerations into its Lagrange dual. The dual is a convex optimization problem over constraint forces, which also play the role of Lagrange multipliers for the primal problem." (doc/computation/index.rst:1023.)

In other words, contact forces are the Lagrange multipliers of the linear/conic constraints in the primal. They emerge from the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions, not from the optimization variables. The same physical answer can also be obtained by writing the Lagrange dual problem, in which forces are the primary variable. The doc gives the dual explicitly:

\[ \begin{aligned} f \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\lambda}\;\; & \tfrac{1}{2}\, \lambda^{T} (A + R)\, \lambda \;+\; \lambda^{T} (a_{u} - a_{r}) \\[4pt] \text{subject to}\quad & \lambda \in \Omega \end{aligned} \]

Source: references/mujoco/doc/computation/index.rst, line 1251 (eq. :label: eq:dual). Auxiliary quantities: \(A = J M^{-1} J^{T}\) is the inverse inertia in constraint space; \(a_{u} = J M^{-1}(\tau - c) + \dot{J}\, v\) is the unconstrained acceleration in constraint space.

Reading this:

  • \(\lambda\) is the optimization variable; at the optimum, \(\lambda = f\), the constraint force vector.
  • \(\Omega\) is the constraint set on \(\lambda\): equality multipliers \(\lambda_{\mathcal{E}}\) are unconstrained; friction-loss \(\lambda_{\mathcal{F}}\) sits in a box \(|\lambda_{\mathcal{F}}| \le \eta\); contact \(\lambda_{\mathcal{C}}\) sits in the friction cone \(\mathcal{K}\) itself (not the dual — the cone is the dual of the dual). For pyramidal cones this reduces to \(\lambda_{\mathcal{C}} \ge 0\); for elliptic, it's a second-order cone constraint.
  • The cost is a quadratic in \(\lambda\) with positive definite Hessian \(A + R\). Once \(\lambda\) is found, the acceleration is recovered from \(M\,\dot{v} = \tau - c + J^{T}\lambda\) — standard Newton's-law bookkeeping.

So forces sit in the dual; accelerations sit in the primal. Neither contains both. MuJoCo's solvers exploit this: the Newton and CG solvers attack the (reduced) primal, while the PGS solver attacks the dual. Both produce the same \((\dot{v}, f)\) pair at convergence.

This is what the phrase "force is a Lagrange multiplier" means concretely: in MuJoCo, you don't directly compute a force from a material law — you set up an optimization in acceleration space, and whatever force value makes the KKT conditions hold is the contact force. Drake's SAP flips this: \(\gamma\) (the impulse) appears explicitly as a derivative of the cost function, computed from a constitutive law. We'll see this contrast crystallize in the SAP block next.

The constitutive-based problem (Drake / SAP)

Drake's SAP solver minimizes a primal cost in velocity:

\[ \begin{aligned} v \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{v}\;\; & \tfrac{1}{2}\, (v - v^{*})^{T} A\, (v - v^{*}) \;+\; \sum_{i} \ell_{i}\!\left(v_{c_i}\right) \\[4pt] \text{where}\quad & v_{c_i} = J_{i}\, v, \qquad \gamma_{i} = -\,\frac{\partial \ell_{i}}{\partial v_{c_i}} \end{aligned} \]

Source: references/drake/multibody/contact_solvers/sap/sap_model.h:368: "primal cost \(\ell_{p}(v) = \tfrac{1}{2}(v - v^{*})^{T} A (v - v^{*}) + \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma^{T} R\, \gamma\)" — the right-hand sum is the regularizer cost, expressed per-constraint as \(\ell_{i}(v_{c_i})\); see sap_constraint.h:24.

Reading the symbols:

  • \(v\) is the unknown post-timestep generalized velocity (the optimization variable).
  • \(v^{*}\) is the free-motion velocity: what \(v\) would be if all constraints were removed (sap_contact_problem.h:35).
  • \(A\) is an SPD approximation of the linearized discrete dynamics matrix, block-diagonal across "cliques" of bodies (sap_contact_problem.h:75).
  • \(J_{i}\) is the \(i\)-th constraint's Jacobian, mapping generalized velocity to constraint velocity \(v_{c_i}\).
  • \(\ell_{i}(\cdot)\) is the \(i\)-th constraint's convex per-constraint cost function (sap_constraint.h:24). Each constraint type provides its own \(\ell\) — friction-cone, distance, Hunt-Crossley dissipation, hydroelastic, etc.
  • \(\gamma_{i}\) is the constraint impulse, defined as the negative gradient of \(\ell_{i}\) with respect to constraint velocity (sap_constraint.h:33).

At the optimum, the gradient of the primal cost is zero, which gives the discrete momentum balance:

\[ A\,(v - v^{*}) \;=\; J^{T} \gamma \]

Source: references/drake/multibody/contact_solvers/sap/sap_contact_problem.h:32, eq. (1).

In words: the constrained velocity \(v\) is the one closest (in the inertia-weighted sense) to free flight, while paying a convex penalty \(\ell_{i}\) per constraint. The contact impulses come out as gradients of the cost function, not as the outputs of an explicit cone-membership constraint — notice the SAP problem has no constraint set; it's unconstrained over \(v\). This is what the SAP paper's title, "An Unconstrained Convex Formulation," is pointing at. For a hydroelastic contact, the cost \(\ell\) is constructed from the upstream pressure field and Hunt-Crossley dissipation; the resulting impulse equals what the integral \(\int_{S} p(x)\, \hat{n}(x)\, dA\) predicts. The physical material enters through \(\ell\).

Reading the equations side by side

The two problems look genuinely different:

  • Variable space. MuJoCo solves over acceleration (with a slack variable). SAP solves over velocity. For a discrete-time integrator they're related by \(\Delta t\); the choice affects the Hessian's conditioning but not what's expressible.
  • Constraint structure. MuJoCo has explicit cone-membership constraints (\(J\,x - y \in \mathcal{K}^{*}\)). SAP has no explicit constraints — the cone behavior is baked into the convex cost \(\ell_{i}\). SAP is solving an unconstrained convex optimization; MuJoCo a constrained one.
  • Where physical knowledge enters. In MuJoCo, physics enters through \(R\) and \(a_{r}\) — computed from solref/solimp, which are solver hyperparameters in disguise. In SAP, physics enters through \(\ell_{i}\) — computed from upstream material parameters (Young's modulus, friction coefficient, dissipation in physical units). Both problems are convex; both produce contact forces satisfying Newton's laws and friction at the optimum. The distinction is where the physics is encoded.

5. What's actually the same across all three

It's worth noting where the three engines genuinely agree, because the differences are louder than the similarities and it's easy to overstate them:

  • Broadphase → narrowphase → constraint setup → solve. All three engines split contact into the same four stages. The boundaries are the same; what's inside each stage differs.
  • GJK + EPA for general convex pairs. All three use GJK to test for intersection and EPA to extract penetration depth and normal under penetration. Drake, via FCL; MuJoCo and PhysX, in-house. The math is identical.
  • Coulomb friction. All three implement Coulomb friction with a per-pair coefficient (or two: static and dynamic). They differ in whether the cone is pyramidal, elliptic, or smoothed; not in whether it's Coulomb.
  • Discrete time stepping by default. All three are designed around a fixed-step discrete update of the dynamics, not around adaptive ODE integration. Drake nominally supports continuous-time mode but in practice everyone uses the discrete plant.
  • Convex solvers, ultimately. MuJoCo's Newton and Drake's SAP are both convex primal/dual Newton-style solvers. PhysX's PGS/TGS, while iterative-impulse rather than Newton-style, is solving a problem that's also convex (LCP with a positive- semidefinite system matrix). The convexity story is universal in this generation of engines.

So the question is rarely "which engine produces correct contact" — all three produce defensible answers in their domain — but rather "which engine answers the kind of question you're asking."

6. Where the philosophies push in different directions

The interesting differences are downstream of the constraint-versus- constitutive split. Four pressure points, in roughly increasing depth.

6.1. What "softness" actually means

In MuJoCo, "soft contact" means regularized constraint enforcement: instead of a hard non-penetration inequality, the solver allows a little penetration in exchange for a numerically nicer optimization, with solref/solimp controlling how much. The "softness" exists to keep the convex problem well-conditioned, not to model material compliance.

In PhysX, "soft contact" via the negative-restitution back door is the same idea in different clothes: a regularized constraint where the user picks a stiffness, but with no constitutive grounding.

In Drake's hydroelastic, "soft" means the geom literally has a finite-stiffness pressure field. A 1 MPa silicone sphere produces a specific traction distribution at a specific penetration. There's no solver hyperparameter to tune; you specify the modulus and the geometry, and the force comes out.

The mapping K·r ↔ p(x)·A is mathematically possible — you can pick a solref that reproduces the steady-state force of a given hydroelastic contact — but it's a coincidence at the level of one static frame. The transient compliance, the way the contact patch evolves under sliding, none of that comes along for the ride.

6.2. Smoothness of contact birth and death

In MuJoCo and PhysX, contact is a discrete event. A point either exists in the contact list or it doesn't. As a body rolls across an edge, contacts pop in and out; as a body lifts off, the contact vanishes. PhysX's persistent manifold smooths this somewhat by reusing identities across frames, but the underlying transition is still discrete.

In Drake's hydroelastic, the contact surface is a continuous function of state. As a foot rolls across a curved floor, the patch deforms continuously and the integrated force varies smoothly. There is no "contact point appeared, contact point disappeared" event — the integrand is continuous in configuration.

This matters in exactly one circumstance: when you want to differentiate through the dynamics. The hard contact birth/death events in the constraint pipelines limit gradient-based methods. The smooth contact surface in hydroelastic doesn't. For everything else — RL, MPC, plain forward simulation — the practical difference is smaller than the marketing suggests, because the discrete-contact engines are designed to absorb the discontinuity through their solver formulation. But for diffsim or gradient-based trajectory optimization, this is a real and structural difference.

6.3. Distributed force

When a flat surface rests on another flat surface, the contact is a distributed force, not a point. Each engine handles this differently:

  • MuJoCo hand-codes box-box to return up to 8 points, and uses multiCCD — a perturbation probe that rotates each geom by ±0.001 rad and re-runs the penetration query — to surface up to 5 contacts for general convex pairs. True point contacts (sphere, ellipsoid) stay at 1.
  • PhysX takes a fundamentally different route to the same destination: when the persistent manifold needs a full regenerate, it runs SAT to pick the contact axis, finds the incident face, and clips it against the reference face in 2D (Sutherland-Hodgman style) to produce up to 4 contacts in one shot. Most frames the manifold is reused with only a single GJK refresh on top.
  • Drake doesn't have to do anything special. The contact surface is the distributed contact, and force is integrated over it.

This is one of the cleanest places where hydroelastic's win is real and not merely cosmetic. Edge contact, corner contact, conformal contact — all handled by the same pressure-field integral, with no special cases. In the constraint-based engines, these are ongoing engineering challenges papered over with carefully-tuned narrowphase routines.

6.4. Where the material parameters live

This is the cleanest test for which philosophy an engine implements: where does the user specify "this is silicone"?

  • MuJoCo: on the contact (or geom default), via solref and solimp. Numerical parameters with units that mostly happen to look physical (a "time constant" in seconds), but their job is solver conditioning.
  • PhysX: on the material, via friction, restitution, and damping. These are coefficients, not material constants in the constitutive sense — restitution is a pure number in [0,1] (or negative for spring mode), not an elastic modulus in Pa.
  • Drake: on the geom's proximity properties, via hydroelastic_modulus in pascals, plus dissipation and friction. The modulus is a material constant; it's consumed unmodified by the contact-surface computation.

None of the three is "right" in some absolute sense. But the question "what numerical value should I pick to simulate silicone?" has very different answers in each. In Drake, you Google silicone's Young's modulus. In MuJoCo, you back-solve solref against a target steady-state force. In PhysX, you accept that you're picking values for behavior, not for materials, and you tune.

7. Where each engine is the right tool

Without crowning a winner, here's the honest read on what each engine is best at, based on what its source code optimizes for:

  • MuJoCo is hard to beat for contact-rich reinforcement learning, model-predictive control, and dexterous manipulation research where you need thousands of rollouts per second on a laptop. The implicit-in-velocity reference acceleration and the convex Gauss-principle formulation let it take 2-millisecond timesteps with stiff contact and not blow up. The tradeoff is that material parameters are solver parameters, and "feels like silicone" is a tuning problem.
  • PhysX is best when you want general-purpose, well-tested rigid-body physics with broad geometry support and the option of GPU acceleration (especially via Isaac Sim / Isaac Lab). It's the most pragmatic of the three: persistent manifolds, iterative impulses, two solver options for stiff content (TGS), four broadphase options to fit different scene profiles. The tradeoff is the same as MuJoCo's — no constitutive contact — plus the friction cone is pyramidal-only, which is a real (small) approximation for dexterous manipulation.
  • Drake is the right tool when you need distributed contact done right out of the box, when you want material parameters to be material parameters, or when you're doing gradient-based methods that need smooth contact. It's also the only one of the three with first-class deformable-body support and clean geometry/dynamics separation in its architecture. The tradeoff: hydroelastic pre-meshing has setup cost, and the discrete-time SAP solver is generally slower per step than MuJoCo's per-island Newton. For workloads that don't need hydroelastic's smoothness or distributed-contact properties, you're paying for capabilities you're not using.

None of these statements would surprise a long-time user of the respective engine. The point of writing them down is that, when you're choosing, the relevant question isn't "which is the best simulator." It's "which question is my problem actually asking?"

9. A short reading list (the whole-series version)

  • Stewart, "Rigid-Body Dynamics with Friction and Impact" (SIAM Review 2000). The textbook framing of constraint-based contact. The mathematical setting MuJoCo and PhysX both inhabit.
  • Todorov, "Convex and analytically-invertible dynamics with contacts and constraints" (ICRA 2014). The paper behind MuJoCo's convex solver formulation.
  • Catto, "Iterative Dynamics with Temporal Coherence" (GDC 2005). The PGS-on-impulses formulation PhysX (and Box2D, Bullet) descends from. Short, very clear.
  • Elandt, Drumwright, Sherman, Ruppel, "A Pressure Field Model for Fast, Robust Approximation of Net Contact Force and Moment Between Nominally Rigid Objects" (IROS 2019). The hydroelastic paper. Read before opening Drake's source.
  • Castro, Permenter, Han, "An Unconstrained Convex Formulation of Compliant Contact" (T-RO 2023). The paper behind Drake's SAP solver.
