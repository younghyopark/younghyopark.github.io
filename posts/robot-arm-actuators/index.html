The Robot Arm Underneath Matters More Than You Think

2026-04-29 · Younghyo Park
A gentle field guide to what's actually inside a robot arm joint — and why those choices silently dictate what kinds of skills are even possible on top of it.

1. Why this matters at all

When you watch a flashy robot demo on Twitter, the part that gets all the attention is usually the policy — the neural network, the VLA, the diffusion-based thing-of-the-month. The arm itself is treated as a kind of given: a generic stick with joints that the smart software drives around.

But spend any time near real hardware and you start to notice that which arm you picked quietly decides what algorithms even make sense. Want to do contact-rich manipulation? Compliant insertion? Imitation learning by physically pushing the arm around? Whether those are easy, hard, or flat-out impossible depends almost entirely on what's bolted into each joint — and most of that is invisible from the outside.

The goal of this post is to crack open one joint and look inside. We'll first do a parts catalog — motor, driver, gearbox, brake, sensor — in the order each piece becomes relevant. Then we'll see how different combinations of those pieces give you fundamentally different robots, even when they all have six joints arranged in a vaguely human-arm shape. By the end you'll know why a roboticist looks slightly pained when someone says "just use a Dynamixel arm to do force control."

2. A parts catalog

Every robot joint is some combination of these five things, in roughly this order from input to output:

motor → driver → gearbox → (optional brake) → output shaft, with sensors sprinkled along the way.

Each of these has a couple of choices, and each choice has consequences.

2.1. The motor

The motor is the bit that actually turns electricity into rotation. For our purposes there's only one distinction worth caring about: brushed vs brushless.

  • Brushed DC motors are the old-school kind. A pair of carbon "brushes" rubs against a rotating commutator inside the motor and physically switches which coils are energized. They're cheap and dead simple to drive — just apply a voltage. Downside: the brushes wear out, they spark, and efficiency is lower. You'll see these in cheap hobby servos.
  • Brushless DC motors (BLDC) — sometimes called PMSMs in textbooks — have no brushes. The coils are in the stationary outer part (the stator), the magnets are on the rotor, and an electronic circuit decides which coil to energize at which moment based on where the rotor currently is. They're more efficient, last essentially forever, and can deliver very high torque density. Almost every serious modern robot joint uses BLDCs.

There's a second axis that matters once you start designing compact joint modules: framed vs frameless.

  • A framed motor is what you'd buy off Amazon: it comes in its own housing, with its own shaft and its own bearings, ready to bolt down. You connect its shaft to whatever you want to drive.
  • A frameless motor is sold as just the two raw parts — the rotor (a ring of magnets) and the stator (a ring of coils). No housing, no shaft, no bearings. You're expected to integrate it into your joint structure: the joint's own housing becomes the motor housing, the joint's own bearings support the rotor. This is annoying mechanically, but it lets you build joints that are dramatically more compact and lighter, because you've stopped paying twice for housing and bearings. Almost every modern humanoid / quadruped joint module uses a frameless BLDC for this reason — the "pancake" motors you see in Boston Dynamics, Unitree, Tesla Optimus joints are all frameless.

2.2. The driver (and what "integrated drive" means)

A brushed motor doesn't really need much — you can drive it with a battery and a switch. A BLDC, on the other hand, can't run without electronics: somebody has to know the rotor angle in real time and switch the three coils in the right sequence. That somebody is the motor driver, sometimes called the ESC, the inverter, or the "drive."

A modern driver does field-oriented control (FOC): it measures the current going into each coil, knows the rotor's angle from an encoder, and continuously commands the right phase currents to produce a smooth, efficient torque. Good FOC drivers run their control loops at tens of kilohertz.

Now, you can either buy your motor and your driver separately and wire them up, or you can buy them as a single unit. When the driver electronics are built into the same housing as the motor (and often the encoder and the gearbox too), people call it an integrated drive. The whole joint becomes a "smart actuator" you talk to over a digital bus — you say "go to position X" or "apply current Y," it does the rest. ROBOTIS's Dynamixels and most humanoid joint modules are integrated-drive units. The opposite would be something like a Maxon motor connected to a separate cabinet of motor controllers, which is what you see on classical industrial arms.

2.3. The gearbox

A motor on its own spins fast and produces relatively little torque. A robot joint wants the opposite — lots of torque, modest speed. The fix is a gearbox (or "reducer") that trades speed for torque. The gear ratio tells you how much trading is happening: a 100:1 gearbox means the motor spins 100 times for every one rotation of the output, and (ignoring losses) the output torque is 100 times the motor torque.

There are three flavors of gearbox you'll meet most often:

  • Planetary gears. The classic: a central "sun" gear, a ring of "planet" gears around it, all inside an outer ring. Cheap, robust, handles high speeds well, easy to stack multiple stages for big ratios. Downsides: noticeable backlash, modest precision, modest shock tolerance. Most affordable smart actuators, including the Dynamixel X-series, use planetary gearboxes.
  • Harmonic drives (a.k.a. strain wave gears). A clever design that uses an elastically deforming flexible spline to mesh teeth in a way that gives near-zero backlash and very high reduction ratios (50:1 to 320:1) in a thin, hollow package. They're the workhorse of high-end industrial arms and cobots. Downsides: expensive, mildly compliant (the flex spline is by definition springy), and they exhibit hysteresis — the relationship between input and output isn't a perfectly clean line, especially when reversing direction.
  • Cycloidal reducers. An eccentric input drives a lobed "cycloidal" disc against a ring of pins; the disc's wobble is converted into a slow, torque-multiplied output. The crucial property is that load is shared across many contact points at once, which makes cycloidal reducers extremely shock-tolerant: drop the robot, slam the joint into a table, and the reducer doesn't shatter the way a planetary or a thin harmonic spline might. They have low (but not zero) backlash and high efficiency. Modern humanoid and high-end research arms use cycloidal reducers heavily — including the new Dynamixel Y-series.

And one piece of vocabulary that comes up constantly: backlash is the small amount of free play you feel when you reverse direction inside any toothed gear. The teeth aren't perfectly mating — there's a tiny gap, and when you flip direction, the input has to traverse that gap before it picks the output back up. Cheap RC servo? Lots of backlash. Backlash matters because it puts a floor on positioning precision and makes torque control miserable near zero crossings, where the gear teeth aren't even touching.

2.4. Backdriveability (the consequence of motor + gearbox)

Now we can name the most important emergent property of any actuator: backdriveability.

Backdriveability is the property of being able to grab the output shaft (the joint, the link, whatever's after the gearbox) and turn it by hand with the motor unpowered. Backdriveability is a spectrum, not a binary. A direct-drive motor — no gearbox at all — sits at the easy end. A worm-gear winch sits at the hard end: it's self-locking by design, so turning the input moves the output, but pushing the output does nothing. Most robot joints sit somewhere in between.

Here's the part that surprised me when I first felt it. Even a 350:1 planetary gearbox is movable by hand, motors off. Grab a Dynamixel-based research arm, kill the power, push the link — the arm moves. It's friction-y, it has detent-y position-dependent resistance from gearbox-reflected motor cogging, and you'd never call it "transparent." But it's not a wall. A human pushes through a few tenths of a Nm of static friction without thinking. This is a totally generic property of planetary gearboxes — they're roughly 70–90% efficient per stage and aren't self-locking, so even a 3- or 4-stage reduction at hundreds-to-one ends up movable.

What gear ratio actually does is push you toward the hard end of the spectrum without taking you all the way there. Three things resist backdriving, in different ways:

  • Reflected motor inertia, which scales with the gear ratio squared. A 100:1 ratio makes the motor's rotor feel 10,000× heavier at the output. This dominates when you try to accelerate the joint quickly — not at the slow speeds humans push at.
  • Reflected motor cogging, which scales linearly with ratio. A small motor's cogging, multiplied through the gearbox, gives you the detent-y feel.
  • Gearbox friction, which depends heavily on type. Planetary: roughly 70–90% efficient per stage, not self-locking. Harmonic drive: stiffer, with hysteresis. Worm gear: deliberately self-locking, the only architecture that's actually a wall.

So when roboticists call a joint "non-backdriveable," they almost always mean "not transparent enough to use as a clean force-sense channel" — not "literally cannot be moved." The latter is rare. The former is what separates Type A arms from Type C arms in practice, and it's the thing the rest of this post is about.

This is also why the high-ratio architecture is the cheap default. High-gear-ratio joints can hold heavy payloads with tiny motors and barely any current, because the gearbox is doing the holding for them. That's why your $20 hobby servo can hold a surprisingly large arm in the air without overheating. You're trading transparency for cheap, efficient torque.

2.5. The brake

Here's a subtle thing that follows directly from the previous section. Even though a high-ratio joint is movable when you push it, the gravity load of the arm itself is much smaller than the force a human applies. So a 200:1 planetary gearbox has enough static friction to hold the arm's weight against gravity when power is cut — it might creep slowly, but it won't crash. The gearbox effectively acts as a passive brake.

But if your joint has a low gear ratio — the QDD case — the friction is small enough that gravity alone overcomes it. Cut the power and the arm collapses under its own weight. That's bad. You also can't safely "park" a QDD robot without keeping every motor energized and burning current.

The fix is an electric brake: a small electromagnetic disc that, by default (when unpowered), clamps onto the rotor and holds it still. Energize the brake, and it releases. This is "fail-safe" in the literal sense — if the robot loses power, the brakes engage automatically and the arm freezes in place rather than crashing onto the floor. You'll see brakes built into essentially every high-end industrial arm and every modern humanoid joint with low gear ratios.

2.6. Sensors: encoders and torque sensors

Last piece. Two sensors matter:

  • Encoder. Measures angular position. Every joint has at least one. The interesting flavors are incremental (counts pulses, has to be re-zeroed on each boot) vs absolute (reads its position immediately at power-on), and single-turn vs multi-turn (does it remember how many full revolutions it's made). Modern smart actuators almost always use multi-turn absolute encoders, sometimes with a tiny backup battery so they remember their position even through power loss.
  • Output torque sensor. Measures the actual torque the joint is exerting, after the gearbox. Usually a strain-gauge ring sandwiched between the gearbox and the link. This is the expensive sensor: it's a precision part, it has to be calibrated, it's electrically delicate, and it's the single most important component that distinguishes "torque-controllable" arms from the rest. Most arms don't have one.

You can also try to infer torque without an output sensor by measuring motor current (current is roughly proportional to motor-side torque), and that estimate is good or bad depending almost entirely on what gearbox sits between the motor and the output. Which leads us to…

3. Three archetypes of arm

The whole parts catalog above collapses, for our purposes, into two questions:

  1. How much gear reduction is between the motor and the output?
  2. Is there a torque sensor on the output side of that reduction?

Different combinations give you fundamentally different robots.

Type Gear ratio Output torque sensor? What you can control Examples
A High (50:1+) No Position only UR5/UR5e, most Dynamixel-based arms, hobby servo arms
B High (50:1+) Yes Position and torque Franka Research 3 (Panda), KUKA iiwa
C Low (~3:1 to 10:1, "QDD") No (but torque is estimable from current) Position and torque MIT Mini-Cheetah-style actuators, Unitree H1/G1 joints, many recent humanoid arms

3.1. Type A — high gear ratio, no torque sensor: "position-controllable only"

This is by far the most common arm in the wild. A small high-RPM motor is paired with a planetary or harmonic gearbox to get the joint torque up to something useful. There is no sensor measuring the actual torque after the gearbox — you just get a position encoder and a current reading on the motor.

The UR5 is the classic example on the industrial side — no torque sensor in any of its joints. You command joint positions, an internal PID drives the motor to track them, and that's that. Researchers have tried to do "sensorless impedance control" by inferring torque from motor current and a dynamic model, but the high gear reduction smears out the relationship between motor current and the actual force at the end-effector. Friction and stiction inside the gearbox eat the signal.

What you give up:

  • True torque control. You can't ask the joint for 1 Nm and trust it.
  • Compliance. If the arm hits something, it doesn't gracefully give way; it grinds against the obstacle, with the position controller fighting harder and harder until something gives.
  • Transparency. You can grab a Type A arm and push it — a Dynamixel-based arm is perfectly movable by hand, even with motors off. What you can't do is feel like the joint isn't there. There's a constant friction floor and detent-y position-dependent resistance from gearbox-reflected motor cogging. The friction is also what holds the arm against gravity for free, which is the property ALOHA-style leader arms rely on.

What you keep: cheap, robust, very precise position tracking, and high payload per dollar. Pick-and-place where contact is incidental? Fine. Anything contact-rich? You're fighting the hardware before you start.

3.2. Type B — high gear ratio with an output torque sensor: "torque-controllable, the expensive way"

Take the same architecture and bolt a real output torque sensor onto each joint. Now you can measure the torque the joint is actually applying, in spite of the gearbox's friction and compliance, and close a control loop around that measurement.

The Franka Research 3 (formerly Panda) is the canonical example: link-side torque sensors on all 7 axes, paired with harmonic drives. The KUKA iiwa family does the same. With this you get:

  • Honest torque control. Command 1 Nm; get 1 Nm.
  • Impedance / admittance control. "Behave like a virtual spring" actually works.
  • Kinesthetic teaching. The arm cancels its own gravity and friction using torque measurements, so you can drag it around with your hand.
  • Excellent contact sensitivity — sometimes better than a wrist-mounted force/torque sensor, since you're measuring at every joint.

The price is, well, the price. You're paying for seven precision torque sensors and the calibration that makes them trustworthy. This is why a Franka costs what it costs.

3.3. Type C — low gear ratio (QDD): "torque-controllable, the proprioceptive way"

The third archetype came out of the legged-robot world — specifically the MIT Cheetah lineage — and is now showing up everywhere on humanoids and modern research arms. The trick is to refuse the high gear ratio. Instead of a tiny motor with a huge gearbox, you use a much larger, high-torque-density BLDC (typically frameless or repurposed from drone hardware) and pair it with a tiny reduction, typically 3:1 to 10:1. This is a quasi-direct-drive (QDD) actuator.

Now look back at the backdriveability section. Reflected inertia and reflected friction scale with the square of the gear ratio, so going from 100:1 to 6:1 reduces them by a factor of nearly 280. The output shaft is suddenly very easy to push. The motor itself has to be bigger to compensate, but the joint feels alive in a way that a high-ratio joint never does.

The other consequence is more subtle: with so little gearbox in the way, the relationship between motor current and output torque becomes clean enough to actually use. Friction is small and well-behaved. So you can read motor current (which the FOC driver is doing anyway) and treat it as a reasonably honest torque sensor, with no extra hardware. The QDD literature calls this "proprioceptive torque sensing."

So a QDD arm gives you:

  • Torque control without a torque sensor. Cheap, lightweight, no calibration drift on a separate part.
  • True backdriveability. You can grab and move the joint trivially.
  • High-bandwidth force control, because there's no harmonic-drive compliance to filter out the high-frequency content.
  • Robustness to impacts. A direct-ish drive doesn't shatter teeth on hard contact.

Tradeoffs: lower peak holding torque per unit mass (no gearbox mechanical advantage), so the motor itself has to be bigger and burns more current to hold static loads. Worse static torque and positioning accuracy — you feel every wiggle of motor cogging at the output, since there's no high-ratio gearbox to average it away. And because the joint is backdriveable, you generally need to add an electric brake to keep the arm safely parked when power is off. That's tolerable for a humanoid joint but expensive for a slow precise pick-and-place arm that spends most of its life holding pose.

4. Case studies

With the vocabulary in place, here's a tour of actuators and arms you'll actually meet in the wild — spanning four orders of magnitude in price — placed on the A/B/C map. The point isn't to be exhaustive. It's to show how cleanly this single split carves up an otherwise confusing market.

4.1. Type A in the wild — high ratio, position-only

Universal Robots UR5 / UR5e / UR10

Brushless motors with high-ratio harmonic drives in each joint, no joint torque sensors, position-tracking PID per axis. Probably the most-used collaborative arm in the world. The "force mode" in URScript and research papers on "sensorless impedance control" are all heroic attempts to extract torque information from motor current and a dynamic model; the harmonic-drive friction smears the signal, so the result is usable but never crisp. Don't try to do contact-rich manipulation on a UR without an external wrist F/T sensor.

Dynamixel X-series (ROBOTIS)

The cylindrical "smart actuator" you see in essentially every budget research arm. Each module is a small cored or coreless DC motor (often a Maxon coreless on the higher-end XW/XD/XH lines, a proprietary motor on XL/XM/XC), a multi-stage planetary gearbox with ~150:1 to 350:1 reduction, an integrated FOC drive, and a position encoder. No output torque sensor, no brake (the high ratio makes the joint non-backdriveable, so it doesn't need one). The "current control mode" controls motor-side torque, but the gearbox smears that into a loose relationship with output torque.

Arms built on smart servos: ALOHA, WidowX, OpenManipulator-X

Stacks of Dynamixel-X (or the cheaper Feetech STS3215 clones in LeRobot's SO-100 / SO-101 designs) chained on a serial bus. The whole arm inherits the actuator's archetype directly — if every joint is Type A, the arm is Type A.

ALOHA is the canonical example of building a useful teleoperation system on Type A hardware. Both leader and follower arms are stock WidowX 250s, every joint a Dynamixel XM430-W350 with a 350:1 planetary — pushable by hand for the reasons we walked through in the parts catalog. The leader's motors are simply off; the operator puppeteers it directly, and the gearbox friction is enough to hold the arm's own weight at most poses, so it doesn't flop around between motions. The follower runs position control on its own Dynamixels and tracks the leader's joint angles. Total cost: a pair of $5k arms.

What ALOHA can't do is feel a contact event from the follower through the leader. There's no torque sensor and no clean current-to-torque mapping back through the 350:1 reduction, so any contact information has to come from cameras or the operator's intuition. That's not a problem ALOHA tries to solve — it's a teleop pipeline for position-replay imitation learning, and on that axis it works great — but it's the reason you wouldn't reach for an ALOHA-style stack to do compliant insertion or polishing.

The very-cheap end: Feetech STS3215, hobby RC servos

Even higher gear ratios, plastic gears in some, brushed motors with no current sensing in others. Position-only, with worse precision and bandwidth than Dynamixels. The reason these have suddenly become research-relevant is LeRobot — at $5–10 per joint you can afford to break things while iterating, which lowers the barrier to trying VLAs in the first place.

4.2. Type B in the wild — high ratio, with output torque sensors

Franka Research 3 (formerly Panda)

7 DOF, harmonic drives on every joint, link-side strain-gauge torque sensors on every joint. The de facto standard for academic manipulation research because of its torque-control fidelity. "Guiding mode" cancels gravity and friction using the torque measurements so you can drag the arm kinesthetically with your hand — this is the canonical demonstration of what a Type B arm gets you that Type A cannot.

KUKA LBR iiwa 7 / 14

The industrial cousin of the Franka, ~$200k+. Same architectural decision: harmonic drives plus per-joint torque sensors. Heavier, stiffer, and certified for industrial human-robot collaboration scenarios in a way the Franka isn't.

Series-elastic alternatives: ANYdrive, HEBI X5/X8

A different way to reach Type B: instead of a strain-gauge ring, put a precision torsion spring between the gearbox and the link, and measure the spring's deflection with two encoders. Spring deflection times known stiffness equals torque. This is a series-elastic actuator (SEA).

ANYbotics's ANYdrive (used in the ANYmal quadruped) is a brushless motor + harmonic drive + series spring; HEBI Robotics's X5 / X8 modules (CMU spinout) are a more affordable, modular take on the same idea. Both give you closed-loop torque control with the bonus that the spring physically protects the gearbox from impacts. Tradeoff: the spring limits torque bandwidth at high amplitudes, which is part of why Katz's QDD thesis explicitly avoided SEAs.

4.3. Type C in the wild — quasi-direct drive

MIT Mini Cheetah actuator (Ben Katz, 2018)

Almost every modern QDD module — the cloned Mini-Cheetah actuators on AliExpress, the open-source designs powering hobbyist quadrupeds and research humanoid arms — descends from one document: Ben Katz's MIT master's thesis, "A Low Cost Modular Actuator for Dynamic Robots" (advised by Sangbae Kim). The concrete numbers make the QDD philosophy a lot less abstract:

  • Motor: the rotor and stator from an off-the-shelf $60–90 brushless drone motor (a T-Motor U8 equivalent), with the stock housing thrown away — effectively turning a hobby BLDC into a frameless motor. 81 mm air-gap diameter, 21 pole pairs, optimized for torque density rather than RPM.
  • Gearbox: a single-stage 6:1 planetary reduction, made entirely from stock Misumi and KHK gears. About 0.005 rad (0.28°) of backlash, which Katz says is fine for legged locomotion.
  • Torque sensing: none. Torque is controlled by FOC current control on the motor. ~10% static torque error from gearbox friction.
  • Whole-actuator specs: 480 g, 96 mm OD × 40 mm thick, 17 Nm peak torque, 6.9 Nm continuous, current-control bandwidth ~4.5 kHz at low torque, total BOM around $300.

The thesis introduction has the cleanest one-paragraph statement of the QDD philosophy you'll find anywhere:

"We take the same approach to actuation as the MIT Cheetah robots: using high torque density motors and low-ratio, backdriveable transmission to allow high bandwidth torque control. … Each actuator requires only one position sensor (at the rotor of the electric motor), no additional compliant elements built into the actuator, and relatively simple transmissions. Torque control is achieved simply by current control of the electric motor."

Note what Katz explicitly says is not a priority: "positioning accuracy, backlash, and static torque accuracy." For a quadruped doing 6 m/s running and backflips, sub-degree positioning at the foot doesn't matter, because the rest of the state estimation has bigger errors anyway. This is the part most people miss when assuming QDD is universally better.

T-Motor + ODrive (the DIY route)

A frameless or pancake BLDC from T-Motor (U8, U10, U12) paired with an external FOC controller like ODrive, moteus, or VESC. No integrated drive, no integrated brake, no integrated anything — you build the joint yourself. Common in research labs that want a custom QDD module without designing one from scratch, and the natural starting point if you read the Katz thesis and want to try it.

MyActuator RMD-X, CubeMars AK series, SteadyWin GIM

Sealed Chinese-manufactured QDD-style modules: frameless BLDC + planetary or cycloidal reducer + integrated FOC drive + CAN bus, sold as a single unit. Ratios typically 6:1 to 36:1. These have become the default choice for budget humanoid and quadruped builds in 2024+ because they give you 90% of a Mini Cheetah module without the machining work.

Unitree quadruped and humanoid joints

Built around frameless BLDCs with planetary or cycloidal reducers, integrated FOC, and a daisy-chained CAN bus. Used internally on Unitree's quadrupeds (Go1/2, B1/2) and humanoids (H1, G1). The H1 joint modules in particular are often cited as one of the more torque-dense COTS QDD options — part of why so many academic humanoid efforts in the last two years have just bought a Unitree platform off the shelf.

4.4. The in-between: things that don't quite fit A or C

Dynamixel Y-series (ROBOTIS)

ROBOTIS's newer, premium line, designed explicitly for physical-AI and humanoid use cases. Looking inside one is a tour of every term from the parts catalog: a frameless BLDC, an integrated drive ("DYD"), a cycloidal reducer, a multi-turn absolute encoder with optional backup battery, and an optional electric brake. Two reducer ratios, 51:1 and 99:1 — meaningfully lower than the X-series's 150–350:1, but still much higher than QDD's 3–10:1. Modular SKUs (M001, B001, R051, R099, A051, A099) let you mix and match motor, brake, and reducer.

Crucially, the Y-series still has no output torque sensor. On paper it's still Type A. In practice it's a Type A trying as hard as it can to feel like Type C without giving up the holding-torque advantage of a moderate gearbox. It powers ROBOTIS's OMY manipulator and AI Worker platform, both aimed at imitation- and reinforcement-learning workflows.

Aside: this in-between zone is getting crowded

As humanoids and physical-AI workflows become a meaningful fraction of actuator demand, more vendors are quietly drifting toward the same recipe as the Y-series: frameless BLDC, cycloidal reducer, ratio in the 20–100:1 range, optional brake, no output torque sensor. It's cheaper than a true Type B (no precision torque sensor on every joint) while keeping more holding torque than a true QDD. Whether this actually feels like Type C in practice depends almost entirely on how cleanly the gearbox transmits motor current — and that's where the cycloidal reducer matters.

5. So what?

Here's the part I find genuinely worth dwelling on.

When people argue about which control scheme, which policy class, which data-collection method is "the right one" for manipulation, the argument is almost always made as if hardware is fungible. It isn't. Some examples of how the actuator quietly decides things upstream:

  • Imitation learning by puppeteering. All three types can be moved by hand, but only Type B and Type C feel transparent enough to grab and walk directly through a trajectory on the actual task arm. Type A arms work great in leader-follower configurations (ALOHA-style, leader motors off, follower tracking position) — but you don't get force feedback from the follower's contact events, so the demonstration distribution is fundamentally different.
  • Reinforcement learning on real hardware. Type A arms eat contact like a brick wall — collisions are expensive in both senses. Type C arms forgive a lot, which is why so much manipulation RL on real hardware happens on QDD-actuated platforms.
  • Sim-to-real. Modeling a high-ratio gearbox with nonlinear friction and stiction faithfully in simulation is its own research career. Modeling a low-ratio direct-ish drive is comparatively easy. Your sim-to-real gap depends on which arm you bought.
  • Safety around humans. A Type A arm running into your hand will keep pushing until something trips a fault. A Type B or Type C arm can detect contact in milliseconds and stop, or even comply.
  • What "skills" are even feasible. Polishing, wiping, insertion, assembly — anything where you regulate force against a surface — is much easier on B or C than on A.

The takeaway isn't that any of these archetypes is "best." They aren't. Each is optimal in its own region of the cost / capability space. The takeaway is that the actuator architecture is upstream of nearly every algorithmic decision you'll make on top of the arm, and pretending otherwise leads to a lot of wasted effort.

If you're new to robotics and trying to figure out why a paper "doesn't reproduce on your arm," check this layer first. Often it's not the policy. It's that the arm in the paper had a torque sensor, and yours doesn't, and no amount of fine-tuning is going to close that particular gap.
