Inside MuJoCo's Contact Pipeline (and Why It's Not Drake's)

2026-05-06 · Younghyo Park
A ground-up tour of how MuJoCo's contact simulation actually works — broadphase, narrowphase, solref/solimp, the convex solver — and why Drake's hydroelastic contact model answers a different question entirely. The point of the comparison isn't to declare a winner. It's to make the philosophical split underneath each engine visible, so the right tool for your problem stops feeling like a matter of taste.

1. Why care what's under the hood

If you've used MuJoCo for more than a few weeks, you've fought with contact. A box rests on a table and slowly drifts sideways. A finger pushes into a cube and the cube shoots away with twice the energy you put in. Someone tells you to "tune solref and solimp" and you go read the docs and come back more confused than when you started. The numbers don't have units you recognize. The five-number solimp tuple is a piecewise function with a midpoint and an exponent. What does any of this have to do with contact?

Meanwhile, your colleague who uses Drake casually mentions "hydroelastic contact" like it's a feature you can just turn on, shows you a video where a deformable foot rolls smoothly over a curved floor with continuous force traces, and asks why MuJoCo doesn't have it. You go look it up, find people in GitHub issues saying "you can fork MuJoCo and add it," and wonder if that's actually a good idea.

This post tries to answer that. The short version is that the question "why doesn't MuJoCo have hydroelastic contact" is a category error: the two engines aren't picking different points on the same spectrum, they're answering different questions. To see why, you have to understand what MuJoCo's contact pipeline actually does — not as an API, but as a sequence of mathematical decisions, with code you can look at. So that's what we'll do first. Then we'll look at hydroelastic contact, and the friction between the two will be obvious.

Throughout, I'll cite specific files and line numbers from the MuJoCo source (the google-deepmind/mujoco repo) so you can go follow along. None of this is secret — it's all in there, and the comments are good. It's just spread across enough files that nobody assembles it for you.

2. Two philosophies of contact

Before we get to code, the single most useful thing to internalize is that there are two genuinely different ways to think about what contact is, in a physics simulator.

2.1. Contact-as-constraint

The first view says: contact is a constraint. When two rigid bodies touch, the universe imposes a rule on them — "you cannot interpenetrate" — and the simulator's job is to find whatever forces are needed right now to make the rule hold while everything else in the dynamics keeps doing its thing. The forces themselves are not derived from a material model. They're Lagrange multipliers: whatever values it takes to keep the constraint satisfied. Friction sits on top of this as another constraint ("the tangential force must lie inside the friction cone") that also gets enforced by some impulse the solver works out.

This view is the lineage of Stewart, Trinkle, Anitescu, Potra, Erleben — "rigid body contact dynamics" — and it leads naturally to a constrained optimization problem each timestep: given the bodies' inertias and external forces, find the contact forces that satisfy non-penetration, the friction cone, and Newton's laws. MuJoCo lives in this world. So does Bullet, ODE, PhysX, Brax (sort of), and most of what you'd recognize as a "rigid body simulator."

2.2. Contact-as-constitutive-law

The second view says: contact is a constitutive law. The bodies aren't rigid — they're slightly squishy, and what we call "contact" is really a small region where they overlap and push back on each other with some pressure that depends on how much they overlap. Specify the pressure law (a stress-strain relationship of the material), integrate it over the overlapping region, and you get a force. The simulator then plugs this force into the equations of motion the same way it plugs in gravity or a spring.

This view is the lineage of finite element method (FEM) people treating contact as a constitutive boundary condition, and its cleanest modern crystallization is Drake's hydroelastic contact model (Elandt, Drumwright, Sherman, Ruppel 2019). Instead of rigid bodies bumping into each other, you give every geom a "pressure field" — a scalar function that's zero on its surface and grows as you go inward — and at any given configuration, two bodies in contact share an equilibrium surface where their pressure fields balance. The force is the integral of the pressure over that surface, and the friction is a regularized Coulomb law layered on top of the prescribed normal traction.

2.3. The dual lens

Here's the cleanest way to hold both views in your head at once, and it'll matter when we contrast the two engines later:

  • Constraint-based contact answers: "given current state and inertia, what set of contact impulses is consistent with non-penetration and the friction cone?" Force is what the solver decides it is.
  • Constitutive contact answers: "given current state, what is the pressure at every point on the contact surface?" Force is fully prescribed by state, and the equations of motion just receive it.

Both are valid. They aren't even strictly incompatible — they're dual ways of describing the same phenomenon, and in certain limits they agree. But the engines that grow out of each viewpoint look very different inside, optimize different quantities, and have different failure modes. With that in mind, let's open MuJoCo.

3. MuJoCo's contact pipeline, end to end

At the highest level, every MuJoCo timestep does these three things, in order:

  1. Find contacts. Figure out which geoms are touching or about to touch, and where.
  2. Build the constraint problem. Turn that list of contacts into a numerical optimization problem in which the contact forces are the unknowns.
  3. Solve it. Use a convex solver (Newton, CG, or PGS) to find contact forces and the resulting accelerations self-consistently.

We'll walk through each. The relevant source files in the MuJoCo repo are in src/engine/. Let's go.

3.1. Step 1a — Broadphase: which pairs even need looking at?

A scene with N geoms has N(N−1)/2 possible pairs. Almost none of them are touching. The first job of collision detection is to throw out the obvious non-collisions cheaply, before doing any expensive geometry work.

MuJoCo uses a covariance-aligned sweep-and-prune algorithm. The trick: compute the principal-axis frame of all the geoms (an eigendecomposition of their position covariance matrix), project every geom's bounding sphere onto that frame's longest axis, sort by projected position, and only check pairs whose projections overlap. This is a one-shot rotation per timestep that aligns the sweep direction with the scene's longest spread — tall stacks get swept vertically, wide tables get swept horizontally — and it makes the linear sweep much more discriminative than a fixed world-axis sweep would be.

You can see it being computed here:

src/engine/engine_collision_driver.c

// construct covariance-aligned 3D frame
mju_eig3(eigval, frame, quat, cov);

The output of broadphase is a flat array of "this pair survived, go check it carefully" — a few thousand candidate pairs in a typical robot scene, down from the naive quadratic count.

3.2. Step 1b — Narrowphase: where are the contact points?

For each surviving pair, MuJoCo dispatches to a type-specific collision routine. There's a 2D function table indexed by (geom_type_a, geom_type_b) — e.g. (sphere, plane), (capsule, box), (box, box). For primitive pairs, there's a hand-written closed-form routine in engine_collision_primitive.c that returns the contact point, normal, and penetration depth analytically. For non-primitive pairs — meshes, convex hulls, anything not on the closed-form list — the dispatch falls through to the general-purpose GJK + EPA pipeline in engine_collision_gjk.c.

GJK (Gilbert-Johnson-Keerthi) is the standard textbook algorithm for testing whether two convex shapes intersect; EPA (Expanding Polytope Algorithm) is the followup that, given that they do intersect, finds the contact normal and penetration depth. Together they handle the long tail of geometry that doesn't fit a primitive case. There's also a separate path for SDF-based collisions in engine_collision_sdf.c for geoms that have a signed-distance function defined.

Two details worth surfacing here, because they show up later in the philosophical contrast with hydroelastic:

(a) Multi-contact for some primitive pairs. Most narrowphase routines return one contact point per pair. The exception is box-box, in engine_collision_box.c, which can return up to 8 contact points for a single pair. That's because a box resting flat on another box doesn't have a unique "contact point" — it has a face. If you returned only one point, the resting box would teeter and drift. So the box-box collider explicitly enumerates the corners of the overlapping region and emits a contact at each, giving the solver enough information to pin all the rotational degrees of freedom. This is the discrete-contact pipeline doing its best impression of a distributed force, and it's worth holding onto: it's a hint that even MuJoCo's own developers know "one point per pair" is sometimes too few.

(b) Contacts are events. Every contact MuJoCo finds in this step is added to the mjData.contact array via mj_addContact. If two bodies are separating — about to lose contact — the contact simply isn't added on the next timestep. There's no smooth birth/death of contact patches. Contacts appear when the narrowphase reports penetration (or proximity, given a margin) and disappear when it doesn't. This is fine in the rigid-body world; it'll matter later.

3.3. Anatomy of an mjContact

Once the narrowphase has finished, we have a list of contacts. Let's pause and look at what one of them actually contains, because this struct is where MuJoCo's whole philosophy gets baked in:

include/mujoco/mjdata.h

struct mjContact_ {
  mjtNum  dist;                    // distance; negative = penetration
  mjtNum  pos[3];                  // contact midpoint
  mjtNum  frame[9];                // normal [0-2], tangents [3-8]

  mjtNum  friction[5];             // [0-1] = mu, [2] = spin, [3-4] = roll
  mjtNum  solref[mjNREF];          // solver reference (normal)
  mjtNum  solreffriction[mjNREF];  // solver reference (friction)
  mjtNum  solimp[mjNIMP];          // solver impedance

  int     dim;                     // 1, 3, 4, or 6
  int     geom[2], flex[2], elem[2], vert[2];
};

Read this carefully. There is no force in this struct. There is no pressure, no contact area, no material property. A mjContact is a geometric object, not a physical one: it tells the solver "two bodies are this far into each other at this point, here's the local frame, here's how squishy it should feel, here's the friction coefficient." Whatever force comes out of the contact later is the solver's problem. That fact — that the contact carries regularization parameters rather than material parameters — is the heart of the constraint view of contact. We'll come back to this.

3.4. Step 2 — solref and solimp: the soft-contact spring

Now the work of turning these geometric contacts into a numerical optimization problem. This step lives in engine_core_constraint.c.

MuJoCo's "soft contacts" are regularized — meaning instead of a hard non-penetration constraint (distance ≥ 0), the solver enforces a softened version, modeled as a virtual spring-damper that pushes bodies apart in proportion to how deeply they've penetrated, with some damping to take energy out. The two parameter families solref and solimp together control exactly what this virtual spring-damper looks like. Once you know what they're parameterizing, the names start making sense.

solref: time constant, damping ratio

solref is two numbers. In its standard mode (the positive-valued case), they describe a damped second-order system:

  • solref[0] = time constant (seconds): how quickly does the spring restore equilibrium?
  • solref[1] = damping ratio (dimensionless, typically 1 = critically damped): how much does it oscillate?

The defaults are solref = (0.02, 1) — a 20 ms time constant, critically damped. The reason these are the parameters (rather than a stiffness k and damping b) is that they're scale-invariant in the right way: a 20-ms-and-critically-damped contact looks the same whether the body weighs a kilogram or a ton, because the solver internally converts to k and b values that account for the body's effective inertia in the constraint direction. From the code: 

// Standard mode (solref[0] > 0):
//   K = 1 / (d_max² · timeconst² · dampratio²)
//   B = 2 / (d_max · timeconst)

where d_max is one of the solimp numbers (we'll get there). If you set solref[0] negative, you've opted into "direct mode" and you're specifying raw stiffness and damping values explicitly. Most of the time you don't want to.

solimp: the impedance shape

solimp is five numbers, and on first read they look like alphabet soup. The function being parameterized is the impedance function d(r): given how deep we currently are into the contact (penetration depth r), how strongly should the constraint be enforced? d ranges from 0 (basically not enforced, squishy) to 1 (basically rigid). The five numbers:

  • solimp[0] = d_min — impedance at zero penetration (just touching). Default 0.9.
  • solimp[1] = d_max — impedance at deep penetration. Default 0.95.
  • solimp[2] = width — how wide a band of penetration depth does the transition occupy. Default 0.001 m.
  • solimp[3] = midpoint — where in [0,1] of that band the transition is centered. Default 0.5.
  • solimp[4] = power — how steeply d rises through the transition (a piecewise polynomial). Default 2 (quadratic).

The function lives at engine_core_constraint.c:2014 in getimpedance. The big idea is "soften when barely touching, harden as you penetrate more," and the five parameters describe a smooth bump function that interpolates between d_min and d_max across a band of width width.

Here's what's important to absorb: none of this is a material model. Nowhere are you specifying that the contact is silicone or steel. solref is a numerical conditioner for the solver — "make the spring stiff enough that bodies don't visibly interpenetrate, soft enough that the optimizer doesn't blow up." solimp shapes how aggressively the regularization kicks in as penetration grows. These are solver hyperparameters, dressed up as physical ones.

This is also why getting MuJoCo to "feel like silicone" is fiddly. There's no E-modulus knob. You back-solve solref and solimp until the steady-state contact force matches what you'd expect from a Young's modulus calculation, but the transient compliance — how the material vibrates and dissipates — can still be wrong, and the engine doesn't care. It's a convex optimizer, not a constitutive law.

3.5. Step 2 (continued) — The reference acceleration

Once we've turned the spring-damper parameters into K and B values, MuJoCo computes a reference acceleration for each constraint:

aref = −B · v − K · r

where v is the velocity of the bodies in the contact's normal direction and r is the position residual (penetration depth, or distance below margin). This is the acceleration the constraint wants — "I want to push the bodies apart at this rate to undo their penetration in the next timestep." The friction constraints get aref with K=0, so they're pure velocity damping ("don't slide tangentially").

Notice this is implicit-in-velocity: the v term is evaluated at the new (post-step) velocity, not the old one. That matters. It means the constraint's damping term is co-solved with the dynamics; it's not an explicit force you slap on top after the fact. This is the secret behind MuJoCo's ability to run stable contact at large timesteps with stiff parameters. We'll need this fact later.

3.6. Step 3 — The convex optimization

All the contact information is now pre-baked. The actual solve happens in engine_solver.c, and it's the most intellectually elegant part of MuJoCo. The problem is stated as Gauss's principle of least constraint: among all motions consistent with the constraints, the actual motion is the one that minimizes the deviation from the unconstrained motion (weighted by inertia).

Concretely, the primal form is something like:

minimize ½ ||x − M−1(τ−c)||²M + (regularizer on slack)
subject to: JExE − yE = 0  (equality)
             JFxF − yF = 0  (friction loss)
             JCxC − yC ∈ K*  (contact, dual cone)

where x is the unknown acceleration, M is the inertia matrix, J is the constraint Jacobian, and K* is the dual of the friction cone. The equation is scary; the picture isn't. We're saying "find the acceleration closest to the free-flight one (weighted by mass), subject to not violating any contacts and staying inside the friction cone on each contact." The Lagrangian dual of this primal problem is a smaller optimization over the contact forces themselves — that's the actual thing the code minimizes.

Three solver flavors live in engine_solver.c:

  • Newton (the default): assembles the full Hessian, factorizes with Cholesky, line-searches on the primal cost. Handles both pyramidal and elliptic friction cones. Fastest for moderate problem sizes.
  • CG (conjugate gradient): nonlinear CG with line search; no setup cost; useful for large or rapidly changing constraint sets.
  • PGS (Projected Gauss-Seidel): block-coordinate descent in dual space — the classical algorithm in this area, included for compatibility and as a cheap warm-start. For elliptic cones, PGS solves a 5D quadratically constrained subproblem at each contact.

The cone choice is independent of the solver: 

ctx->is_elliptic = (m->opt.cone == mjCONE_ELLIPTIC);

Pyramidal cones approximate the friction cone with edges (the classical LCP form). The dual constraints become box constraints — cheap to solve. Elliptic cones use the true second-order cone — physically correct (pyramidal cones over-allow friction along the diagonals), but the dual constraints become second-order, slower per iteration. Elliptic is what you want for serious manipulation; pyramidal is the default because it's faster and "good enough" most of the time.

3.7. Constraint islands and parallelism

One last optimization worth knowing about: not every contact affects every other contact. If your scene has a robot in one corner picking up a cup, and another robot across the room shaking hands with itself, those two contact problems can be solved independently — they share no bodies and no constraints. MuJoCo identifies these constraint islands via a flood-fill on the constraint Jacobian's sparsity pattern (engine_island.c) and farms them out to separate threads. The island decomposition is a small thing, but it's the difference between "real-time on a laptop" and "real-time only on a server" in scenes with many independent agents.

4. Recap: a single timestep, narrated

Pulling it all together, here's what one MuJoCo timestep does to contact, with the names we now have:

  1. Broadphase sweeps geom AABBs along a covariance-aligned axis, drops obvious non-collisions.
  2. Narrowphase dispatches each surviving pair to a primitive routine or GJK/EPA, generating mjContact records with geometric info but no force.
  3. Constraint setup turns solref/solimp/friction into a reference acceleration and an impedance for each contact — the "what the spring-damper wants" part.
  4. Island decomposition splits the contact graph into independent subproblems.
  5. Convex solve, per island, finds contact forces that satisfy Gauss's principle subject to the friction-cone constraint.
  6. Forces map back to accelerations; integrate; repeat.

The thing to keep in mind, for the next section, is that everywhere in this pipeline, "contact" is a discrete geometric event, and "force" is a Lagrange multiplier the solver computes. Nothing in MuJoCo's contact code looks at a material's Young's modulus and computes a pressure. Material-like behavior emerges from the choice of solref/solimp, which are solver hyperparameters that happen to look like a spring-damper.

5. Drake's hydroelastic model, in one diagram's worth of words

Now let's flip to the other camp. I won't reproduce Drake's whole pipeline — this is a MuJoCo post — but the core idea is short enough to state precisely.

In the hydroelastic contact model, every geom is given a pressure field: a scalar function defined on its volume that's zero on the surface, grows linearly inward with some elastic modulus E and depth, and saturates at the geom's interior. Think of it as encoding "if you squished into me, here's how hard I'd push back at this point." A rigid geom has an infinite-stiffness field; a "soft" geom has a finite one.

When two such geoms intersect, the simulator computes the equilibrium surface: the manifold inside the intersection where their two pressure fields are equal. On that surface, define the traction:

p(x) = E · ε(x)

where ε(x) is the strain at point x. Integrate p(x) times the local normal over the equilibrium surface and you have the contact force and moment — not as a Lagrange multiplier, but as a deterministic function of state. Friction is then a regularized Coulomb law on top: take the prescribed normal traction, multiply by μ, smooth the velocity-dependence around zero so it's differentiable. Done.

Two things make this model attractive:

  1. Distributed force, automatically. A foot pressing on a curved floor doesn't generate one contact point; it generates a polygonal patch with a continuous traction across it. Edge contact, corner contact, conformal contact — all handled by the same integral. No hand-tuning the number of contact points.
  2. Smooth gradients. As the foot rolls, the patch deforms continuously. Force varies continuously with state. There's no "contact point appeared, contact point disappeared" event — which is exactly what makes hydroelastic so attractive for differentiable simulation and gradient-based control.

At this point the natural question is "why doesn't MuJoCo just do this?" The answer is that the parts of hydroelastic that make it nice are exactly the parts MuJoCo's pipeline doesn't preserve cleanly. Let's walk through them.

6. Where the philosophies fight

Four points of friction (sorry), in roughly increasing depth.

6.1. Smoothness is lost in the re-discretization

Imagine you wanted to add hydroelastic-like behavior to MuJoCo in the laziest possible way: at each timestep, compute the equilibrium surface as Drake would, then chop it up into a bunch of points, hand each point to the existing mj_addContact as a separate mjContact, and let the convex solver work them out. This is the obvious attempt, and it almost works.

The problem is contact birth and death. As the foot rolls, the equilibrium surface continuously deforms — in Drake, the patch elongates one way and shrinks the other, and the integrated force varies smoothly. In the ported-to-MuJoCo version, the discrete contact points born from that patch appear and disappear as the patch geometry changes. Each contact in MuJoCo is an event (mj_addContact succeeded or it didn't), and the solver experiences abrupt changes in the constraint set as contacts pop in and out.

The buttery gradient that hydroelastic gives you in Drake comes from the integrand being a continuous function of state. Once you hand that integrand to MuJoCo as a list of points, the list itself becomes a discontinuous function of state, and the smoothness is gone. Recovering it would mean adding contact persistence — tracking which contacts in this step correspond to which contacts last step, smoothing their parameters across the transition. MuJoCo's contact pipeline doesn't have that machinery, and bolting it on isn't a small change.

6.2. "Soft" means different things

The word "soft" appears in both worlds, but it's doing different work. In MuJoCo, "soft contact" means regularized constraint enforcement: instead of a hard non-penetration inequality, the solver allows a little penetration in exchange for a numerically nicer optimization problem, with solref/solimp controlling how much. The "softness" exists to keep the convex problem well-conditioned, not to model material compliance.

In hydroelastic, "soft" means the geom literally has a finite-stiffness pressure field. A 1 MPa silicone sphere produces a specific traction distribution at a specific penetration. There's no solver hyperparameter to tune; you specify the modulus and the geometry, and the force comes out.

The mapping K · r ↔ p(x) · A is mathematically possible — you can pick a solref that reproduces the steady-state force of a given hydroelastic contact — but it's a coincidence at the level of one static frame. The transient compliance, the mode shapes, the way the contact patch evolves under sliding — none of that comes along for the ride. There's no clean way for the user to say "this is silicone with E = 1 MPa" and have MuJoCo get the dynamics right; you're back-solving solref to match a constitutive law the engine doesn't believe in.

6.3. Implicit-in-velocity is the secret sauce, and it assumes solver-resolved forces

This one is the deepest, and it's the reason hydroelastic isn't a drop-in even if you solve the contact-persistence problem.

Recall from section 3.5: MuJoCo computes a reference acceleration aref = −Bv − Kr, and the v in there is the post-step velocity. The constraint forces are co-solved with the accelerations: the optimizer simultaneously decides "what contact force λ and what acceleration v̇ are jointly consistent with Newton's laws and the constraint aref?" That joint solve is what lets MuJoCo take large timesteps with stiff contact parameters: the implicit treatment damps out high-frequency modes you'd otherwise have to resolve explicitly.

Now imagine injecting a hydroelastic force into this. The force is a state-dependent prescribed quantity: FHE(q, q̇), evaluated at the current state and added to the dynamics. That's an explicit term. If the hydroelastic material is stiff (high E), the force can change rapidly with state, and you need a small timestep to integrate it stably — exactly the regime MuJoCo's implicit constraint formulation was designed to avoid. A stiff hydroelastic geom in MuJoCo would slow you down to FEM-grade timesteps, which is a death sentence for RL or MPC workloads doing thousands of rollouts per second.

The clean fix is to linearize the hydroelastic pressure law and bake it into the constraint formulation as a Lagrangian penalty — this is roughly what Drake's SAP (Semi-Analytic Primal) integration does. But once you've done that, you've recreated MuJoCo's softening from a different starting point. You haven't really added hydroelastic; you've translated it into the same constraint language MuJoCo already speaks.

6.4. Two-body coupling: equilibrium surface vs solmix

The fourth point is more aesthetic, but it matters for consistency. In hydroelastic, when two soft bodies of different stiffness collide, the equilibrium surface automatically lands closer to the stiffer body, and the combined behavior is symmetric and principled — it falls out of equating the two pressure fields. There's no parameter averaging. The mathematics does it.

In MuJoCo, when two geoms with different solref/solimp collide, the engine has to combine them into a single set of solver parameters for the contact. It does this with a per-geom solmix weight (see mj_contactParam in engine_collision_driver.c): 

mix = solmix1 / (solmix1 + solmix2);
solref[i] = mix*solref1[i] + (1-mix)*solref2[i];

This is fine in practice, but it's a hack. There's no physics behind picking the average, no theorem saying the combined system behaves like a real material with intermediate properties. It's just "we have to come up with one number, here's one." If you ported hydroelastic into MuJoCo's contact pipeline and threw away the equilibrium-surface idea (because MuJoCo wants per-contact solref), you'd have lost one of the things that distinguishes hydroelastic from "many discrete springs." You'd be back to picking a fudge factor, just under a fancier name.

7. So what should you do?

Here's the actually-useful framing, after all of that.

Most of the time, when people say "I want hydroelastic in MuJoCo," what they actually want is one of these:

  • Distributed force on a flat-on-flat contact. The box-box 8-point machinery already covers this for boxes. For other primitives, flex elements (MuJoCo's tetrahedral soft-body primitives) participate in contact natively, and multiccd for mesh-mesh pairs can surface multiple contacts. You can also raise contact margin to widen the activation region. None of this is hydroelastic, but most of the practical wins of hydroelastic for stable manipulation contact are already achievable.
  • Force that doesn't depend on the contact-point count. Tune solref/solimp so that the per-contact force scales reasonably as more contacts are added; use elliptic cones; raise the friction impedance ratio if friction is wonkier than normal forces. The relationship between n contact points and total force is much more controllable than people give it credit for, once you stop treating the parameters as opaque.
  • Edge and corner contact behaving sensibly. This is mostly a meshing problem. Round the edges in your mesh; use convex decomposition; or use SDF geoms for the pieces where edge contact dominates. Hydroelastic does handle edges more gracefully out of the box, but the gap closes a lot once you stop feeding the simulator sharp-cornered convex hulls.

Where the analogy genuinely breaks down — where hydroelastic's win is real and not just polish — is differentiable simulation. If your goal is to backpropagate through dynamics, fit a policy with gradient-based control, or do diffsim-style trajectory optimization, the hard contact birth/death events in MuJoCo's pipeline really do limit you. No amount of clever multi-contact tricks will give you the smooth gradients hydroelastic offers, because the discontinuity is structural — it's in the contact set, not in any one contact's parameters. In that case, the question isn't "can I port hydroelastic into MuJoCo"; it's "should I be in MuJoCo at all," and the honest answer is often "go use Drake, or Brax with soft contacts, or a custom MPM-ish thing built around continuous-contact assumptions."

But for everything else — manipulation realism, contact-rich RL, sim-to-real for grasping — the gap between well-tuned MuJoCo and a hydroelastic engine is much smaller than the marketing suggests. The real win of MuJoCo's pipeline is that the constraint formulation lets you take 2-millisecond timesteps with stiff contact and not blow up, which means you can run thousands of rollouts a second on a laptop. That's upstream of every learning algorithm anyone builds on top, and it's exactly what the implicit-in-velocity, solver-resolved-force view of contact buys you.

So: not the same engine, not the same question, not even the same kind of answer. Knowing which one you actually want is the whole game.

8. A short reading list

If you want to dig further, these are the things I'd actually read, in order:

  • MuJoCo's own doc/computation/index.rst. It's the most coherent technical reference for the constraint solver, Gauss principle formulation, and friction cone choices. It quietly assumes you've already read Todorov 2014.
  • Todorov, "Convex and analytically-invertible dynamics with contacts and constraints" (ICRA 2014). The paper that introduced the convex contact formulation MuJoCo implements. Worth reading before you tune anything.
  • Elandt, Drumwright, Sherman, Ruppel, "A Pressure Field Model for Fast, Robust Approximation of Net Contact Force and Moment Between Nominally Rigid Objects" (IROS 2019). The hydroelastic contact paper. Short, very readable.
  • Drake's hydroelastic user guide. Practical documentation, with diagrams of equilibrium surfaces that finally made the model click for me.
  • engine_solver.c and engine_core_constraint.c in the MuJoCo repo. Read them with the doc page open in another tab. Surprisingly approachable C, and the comments occasionally do the explaining for you.

The point of this post wasn't to make you a contact-simulation expert — that takes years — but to give you the vocabulary to read those things without bouncing off. If solref stops looking like alphabet soup, and the word "constitutive" doesn't seem like an empty buzzword, this was time well spent.
