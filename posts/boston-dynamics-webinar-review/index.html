2. Vision: General-Purpose Humanoids and Economic Rationale

2.1. Why Humanoids, and Why Now?

  • Many customers see the humanoid shape and instinctively assume it can:
    • Perform many types of tasks.
    • Fit into existing human environments without major infrastructure changes.
  • The real appeal is not the human shape per se, but:
    • Flexibility: re-tasking the same machine to different jobs.
    • Adaptability: handling high variability in real-world situations.

2.2. Factory Visits and the "Lighthouse" Task

Alberto's observations from visiting many factories:

  • Tasks are extremely complex: many operations require fine motor skills, multi-step coordination, and dynamic stabilization.
  • Much of this work is still done by humans, even in advanced factories — often because automation is economically unattractive when built as traditional, highly specialized systems.
  • Example: Modern car assembly line — ~1,000 cars per day, many variants, thousands of parts, enormous combinatorial variability.

He defines a "lighthouse" example task:

A worker reaches into a bin of bolts with one hand, picks a bolt, grabs a powered screwdriver with the other hand, inserts the bolt into the tool, then screws it underneath the car roof, possibly using the off-hand to brace the body for balance.

This illustrates: bimanual coordination, whole-body balancing, tool use, and working in awkward, constrained spaces.

2.3. The Goalpost for Humanoids

  • Humanoids aim to directly attack the limitations of hard automation.
  • Alberto's belief: no fundamental reason why such tasks cannot be automated. Hardware is already physically capable. Learning-based methods should be able to scale.

3. Reliability and the Three Pillars of Customer Value

3.1. Reliability Dimensions

From a product perspective, Aya emphasizes three pillars:

  1. Hardware reliability — Robot must physically work for long periods with minimal failures.
  2. Behavior reliability — The robot must perform specific tasks repeatably without frequent errors.
  3. Application software reliability — Higher-level software that orchestrates workflows, handles edge cases, and provides predictable operator experience.

Customers need robots that are predictable and configurable.

4. Three Phases of Product Maturation

Phase 1 – Hardware-Limited Development

  • Main bottleneck: hardware reliability.
  • All humanoids today are in this phase. Atlas is firmly still in Phase 1.

Phase 2 – Learning from Long-Term Deployments ("The Other Bitter Lesson")

  • Robots deployed for extended periods reveal non-obvious product realities: wrong autonomy levels, poor exception handling, misfit interfaces.
  • Alberto calls this "the other bitter lesson": the customer is always right; your initial product ideas are often wrong.

Phase 3 – Large-Scale Hardware Refinement

  • Scaling to thousands of deployed robots reveals rare failure modes.
  • Spot: Phase 3. Stretch: ~two years behind. Atlas: ~two years behind Stretch, still in Phase 1.

5. Why Legs? The Legs vs. Wheels Debate

5.1. Product View (Aya)

  • Legs are not needed everywhere, but the long-term humanoid vision is one robot that can do almost anything a human can do.
  • Key requirement: Maintain positive ROI for customers.

5.2. Engineering View (Alberto)

  • Dynamic stability is no longer the main bottleneck.
  • Capabilities of legged platforms: slimmer profile, faster turning.
  • Total actuator count and complexity can be similar or even higher for wheeled bases.
  • Outstanding challenge: Safety certification for dynamically balancing humanoids.

6. Why Start in Industrial Manufacturing?

  1. Scale — Factories offer large-scale opportunities.
  2. Safety Ramping — Manufacturing environments are structured and amenable to gradually increasing human–robot interaction.
  3. Variability and "Manipulation Completeness" — General assembly lines are close to "manipulation complete".

7. AI Brains, Large Behavior Models, and Training Strategy

7.1. Traditional vs. Learned Behaviors

  1. Classical hierarchical pipeline: perception → planning → control. Problems: maintenance hell, poor scalability.
  2. Learned behavior engines: replace handcrafted structure with neural networks that are trained, not programmed.

7.2. Pre-training vs. Post-training

  • Post-Training: teleoperated demonstrations for each specific task at each customer. High performance but a bottleneck for scaling.
  • Pre-Training: Large central model providing a good initial policy for any new task — a Large Behavior Model (LBM).

7.3. What Does the "AI Brain" Need to Learn?

  1. General locomotion and whole-body movement
  2. Spatial perception and visual understanding
  3. Environment and asset-specific knowledge
  4. Dexterous manipulation and haptics

8. Teleoperation and Data Collection

  • Demonstrator wears a VR headset, sees from Atlas's perspective in stereo 3D, uses body trackers.
  • Typically 5–10 hours of demonstrations per single behavior.
  • Likely future interfaces: language-based task specification, direct visual demonstration.

9. Scaling Training and the Data Flywheel

  1. Deploy to customers.
  2. Collect demonstrations and corrections.
  3. Improve pre-trained model.
  4. Future deployments need less post-training.
  5. Scale to more tasks and sites.
  6. Repeat, compounding capabilities over time.

10. Research Pace and Uncertainties

  • This is likely the fastest period of progress robotics has ever seen.
  • The community is betting that generality will emerge from scale, but full, robust generality has not yet been conclusively demonstrated.

11. Deployment Scale and Timeline

  • BD expects thousands of humanoid robots in industrial environments within 5–10 years.
  • Within 4–5 years, Atlas could be roughly where Spot is today in terms of maturity.

12. Can I Buy an Atlas Now?

  • Atlas is not yet a commercial off-the-shelf product.
  • Hyundai is the first major early partner.
  • Interested organizations should think partnership, not procurement.

13. Gripper Design and Dexterous Manipulation

  • Current hand: three fingers. Sufficient for most near-term goals.
  • Two major bets: RL in Simulation and Learning from Human Hand Use.

14. Overall Takeaways

  • Humanoids (Atlas) are aimed squarely at general-purpose manipulation, especially in automotive manufacturing.
  • BD leverages experience from Spot and Stretch, a structured three-phase maturity model, and teleoperation + simulation-based RL + large behavior models.
  • Short term: industrial manufacturing as the beachhead.
  • Long term: thousands of humanoids within 5–10 years, eventually expanding into homes and other environments.
